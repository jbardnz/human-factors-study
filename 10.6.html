10.6 Atmosphere




10.6.2 State the gases that make up the atmosphere.

In order of abundance the following gases make up the atmosphere:

Nitrogen N2, Oxygen O2 , Water* H2O, Argon Ar , Carbon Dioxide* CO2, Neon Ne, Helium He, Methane* CH4 , 
Hydrogen H2, Nitrous Oxide* N2O, Ozone* O3

* gases with variable composition

While the percentage of nitrogen and oxygen are fairly stable, the amount of greenhouse gases changes and depends on location. Water vapour is extremely variable. In arid or extremely cold regions, water vapour may be nearly absent. In warm, tropical regions, water vapour accounts for a significant portion of atmospheric gases.

Some references include other gases on this list, such as krypton (less abundant than helium, but more than hydrogen), xenon (less abundant than hydrogen), nitrogen dioxide (less abundant than ozone), and iodine (less abundant than ozone).

While oxygen is necessary for most life on Earth, the majority of Earth’s atmosphere is not oxygen. 

Earth’s atmosphere is composed of about 78 percent nitrogen, 21 percent oxygen, 0.9 percent argon, and 0.1 percent other gases. Trace amounts of carbon dioxide, methane, water vapour, and neon are some of the other gases that make up the remaining 0.1 percent.





10.6.4 Describe the variation of pressure as altitude increases. 

Atmospheric pressure is defined as “Force per Unit Area acting on each square meter of surface”. Pressure is measured in Hectopascals (hPa) or Millibars (mbar) in Aviation. (the ISA unit is hPa) - hPa and Mb have the same value.

It is the force exerted on the surface of the earth by the weight of air above that surface.


Imagine a column of air extending from the surface of the earth to the upper limits of the atmosphere. As we ascend through the atmosphere the height of the column of air above us decreases, therefore weighs less and exerts less pressure.

Air in the atmosphere travels from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure.

Changes in Atmospheric Pressure.

The most important factors in causing changes to Atmospheric Pressure are Altitude and Temperature and to a lesser extent Water Vapour.

The influence of Altitude on Air pressure

As altitude increases, atmospheric pressure decreases. It’s important to remember that the pressure at any given altitude in the atmosphere is equal to the weight of the air directly above that point. Remember, at sea level, a column of air will weigh 14.7 pounds per inch – and will therefore exert that much pressure. As we climb through the atmosphere, the air becomes less dense due to the reducing ‘weight’ of the air above. Therefore the air pressure reduces as altitude is increased. 

The rate of this decrease in pressure is not constant. The rate of decrease is much greater near the Earth’s surface where air pressure is high, compared to the upper levels of the atmosphere where air pressure is lower. The rate of change in the pressure as altitude is increased is called the Pressure Lapse Rate. Close to the earth’s surface, the decrease is a decrease of 1 hPa 30 ft but at 20,000 ft it requires a height change of 50 ft to produce a change of pressure of 1 hPa.

Another way of describing this (in the ASPEQ exam)  is that the atmospheric pressure decreases exponentially with altitude— not linearly. At 18,000 ft, the air pressure is about half of what it is at sea level. At 33,500 ft, it is about 25% of sea level pressure. This decrease is non-linear throughout, and does not become linear at any specific altitude.


So as we climb (atmospheric) pressure decreases. The rate at which pressure decreases with an increase in altitude is known as a Pressure Lapse Rate.
  • The standard pressure lapse rate is 1 hPa/30ft. 
  • In the ISA atmosphere sea level pressure is 1013.25 hPa. It can also be expressed (NON ISA) as 29.92 inches of mercury (“Hg) or 14.7 lbs/square inch (PSI).



Because oxygen is about 20 percent of the atmosphere, its presence is responsible for 20 percent of the total sea level pressure.

As you increase in altitude, the percentage of these gasses remains constant, but the volume decreases, (remember from Meteorology), therefore there is less Oxygen available to breath as you increase in altitude. The amount of pressure felt on the body also decreases as altitude increases (from PPL Tech).Partial Pressure of a gas. 

Dalton's law notes the total pressure of a mixture of gases equals the partial pressure exerted by each gas. This concept is important given that the atmosphere is a mixed gas of nitrogen (approximately 78%), oxygen (approximately 21%), and trace other gases.


The independent pressure of each gas is called the partial pressure of that gas. For a pilot, this law demonstrates how increasing altitude results in proportional decreases in the partial pressure of the gas without changing the percentage concentration of that particular gas. 

Oxygen is needed to remain alive. It is combined in the blood stream with nutrients from food, providing the energy essential for life.

Oxygen needs to be supplied to the body at a certain pressure for it to be of any use to us. It must be able to force itself through the lining of the lungs to diffuse into the blood stream. The lungs detect the level of waste carbon dioxide in the lungs and send a message to the brain that controls the amount of breathing required to draw more or less oxygen in.

At sea level the atmospheric air pressure is 760 mm Hg. Because oxygen is about 20 percent of the atmosphere, its presence is responsible for 20 percent of the total sea level pressure.

It is the lowering of the partial pressure of oxygen that causes the medical condition known as Hypoxia at altitude. Additionally, the subsequent decrease of nitrogen’s partial pressure at altitude leads to decreased solubility of the gas. This increases the risk of developing decompression sickness.







This partial pressure of oxygen outside the lungs reduces once breathed in, due to the presence of WATER VAPOUR and CARBON DIOXIDE.

The minimum pressure that the oxygen must be inside the lungs for it to diffuse into the bloodstream is 102 mm Hg.




If there is sufficient oxygen entering the lungs, and all parts of the respiration system are working normally, then sufficient oxygen will reach the body cells. If the body does not receive enough oxygen it will not function properly.

The pressure at sea level is said to be standard at 1013 hPa. As altitude is increased, pressure decreases, as well as temperature (2°C per 1000 feet). 

The partial pressure of oxygen starts to decrease as soon as we leave the ground but does NOT DECREASE SIGNIFICANTLY until 10,000’ AMSL. It is important to note here that the percentage of Oxygen and Nitrogen do not change with an increase in altitude. No matter how high in the atmosphere you go, the percentage will remain the same but the amount of Oxygen and Nitrogen will change.

Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures means:

Oxygen makes up 21% of the atmosphere and accounts for 21% of the total atmospheric pressure.

At sea level, atmospheric pressure is about 760 mmHg; oxygen makes up 21% of this, giving a partial pressure of ~150 mmHg. As altitude increases, the percentage of oxygen remains the same, but the total pressure drops, so the partial pressure of oxygen also drops. At 18,000 ft, atmospheric pressure is about half of sea level (≈ 380 mmHg), so the partial pressure of oxygen is ~75 mmHg — about half the sea level value.

Sea level 21% = 160 mm/Hg partial pressure of oxygen -  Total Pressure = 760 mm/Hg

10,000 feet 21% = 110 mm/Hg - partial pressure of oxygen - Total Pressure = 522.6 mm/Hg

At 18,000ft pressure is ½ of that at sea level

At 34,000ft pressure is ¼ of that at sea level


The PARTIAL PRESSURE of OXYGEN in the BLOOD STREAM REMAINS ALMOST CONSTANT in a CLIMB to 10,000ft AMSL;  BUT REDUCES RAPIDLY CLIMBING HIGHER THAN THAT

At 10,000 ft the partial pressure of oxygen is about half of the value at sea level (approximately 55 MM Hg) and reaches the minimum acceptable level in aviation. Above this altitude without supplemental oxygen Hypoxia symptoms will be experienced (we will discuss this shortly).

To maintain a partial pressure of oxygen equivalent to Sea level conditions a pilot must either be supplied with oxygen through a mask or the cabin pressure must be artificially increased to below 10,000 ft.

By supplying up to 100% Oxygen through a mask, a partial pressure of oxygen within the lungs equivalent to Sea Level can be maintained up to approximately 34,000 Ft. Above that level, even when breathing 100% oxygen, at ambient atmospheric pressure the partial pressure of oxygen within the lungs decreases until at 42,000 Ft it is equivalent to breathing air at 10,000 Ft. At higher levels in order to provide sufficient partial pressure of oxygen the lungs must take in oxygen under pressure ( pressure breathing mask).
