10.18 Vision and Visual Perception



10.18.22 Explain the use of visual cues during landing.

When learning to fly, a student pilot will become familiar with the shape and perspective of the runway.

This is particularly important on the final approach to landing, and pilots soon learn the correct approach perspective of the runway. They also learn how this changes when their approach is made too steeply, or lower than normal.

The ‘correct’ perspective that the student pilot has learnt to adopt can alter under various circumstances and can trick the unwary pilot.



10.18.24 Explain the following visual illusions, 
10.18.26 Describe the methods of avoiding and/or coping with them

(a) autokinesis; 

Autokinetic Effect (autokinesis)

At night a single light source can sometimes appear to ‘dance about’ and move even though it is stationary. A bright star will sometimes appear to be a moving object.

This is called the autokinetic effect and is much more likely if there are no other lights in the vicinity. It occurs because the brain has difficulty in making sense of a single pinpoint of light and will move it around slightly in an attempt to make better sense or some form of pattern out of it.

Methods to Avoid or Prevent Autokinesis

  1. Avoid staring at a single light source.

    Keep your eyes moving with regular, deliberate scanning motions instead of fixating.

  2. Use proper cockpit/flight deck lighting.

    Maintain enough ambient light to prevent your visual system from becoming overly sensitive in darkness.

  3. Look slightly off to the side of the light (off-centre viewing).

    This reduces the chance of triggering the illusion, since autokinesis is most likely when using central vision in dark conditions.

  4. Rely on instruments for orientation and attitude.

    Instruments provide stable, objective information that prevents misinterpretation caused by illusions.

Methods to Cope With Autokinesis Once It Occurs

  1. Shift your gaze or increase scanning.

    Breaking fixation stops the illusion almost immediately.

  2. Cross-check with other visual references or lights.

    This helps confirm that the light itself hasn't moved.

  3. Use instrument references.

    This prevents inappropriate control inputs based on the illusion.

  4. Illuminate the cockpit or increase ambient light if possible.

    More light reduces the sensitivity of the eye that contributes to the illusion.

(b) stroboscopic illumination illusion/flicker vertigo; 

Flicker vertigo can occur when there is a bright light source in the pilot’s field of vision that is being interrupted intermittently but rapidly by some object, usually propeller or rotor blades.

The bright flickering phenomenon can cause the pilot to experience dizziness and feel unwell. It can also lead to convulsions and epileptic fits in some people.

If flicker vertigo is encountered, as it is rare, a pilot should alter their gaze, use a sunshade, or reposition the aircraft in such a way as to avoid the flicker effect.

Flicker vertigo is also caused by flashing lights at a frequency of 4-20 hertz. When a pilot flies through a cloud at night, the strobe lights / anti-collision lights on the aircraft can reflect off the water droplets, creating a flickering effect. This can lead to disorientation, nausea, and even seizures in susceptible individuals. To reduce this effect turn these lights off when in cloud.

(c) the break-off phenomenon; 

When flying in conditions of very little visual, mental or physical stimulation such as long sectors over featureless terrain, and often with a low pilot workload, a phenomenon known as break off can occur.

It is caused by a combination of sensory deprivation, isolation, and the vast, empty view of the earth from high altitude.

Break off is a subjective feeling of the pilot being psychologically ‘detached’ from his or her surroundings. It may be experienced as a feeling of being outside the cockpit looking in.

The pilot may also feel that the aircraft is balancing on a pinhead and about to fall off.

The most effective way to combat it is by re-engaging the mind. By actively increasing your cognitive workload—through tasks like reading, checking flight plans, or simply talking to yourself or a co-pilot—you can overcome the feelings of detachment and monotony. Maintaining radio contact is also a good way to stay connected to the "real world" and break the feeling of isolation.

(d) sector whiteout; 

Whiteout is a weather condition in which visibility and contrast are severely reduced by snow or sand. The horizon disappears completely and there are no reference points at all, leaving the individual with a distorted orientation. Whiteout has been defined as: "A condition of diffuse light when no shadows are cast, due to a continuous white cloud layer appearing to merge with the white snow surface. No surface irregularities of the snow are visible, but a dark object may be clearly seen. There is no visible horizon.

The best course of action is to stop relying on external visual cues and immediately transition to instrument flight, where the aircraft's attitude, altitude, and position are displayed accurately. If the conditions are too hazardous, a go-around or diversion is the safest option.

IN NOVEMBER 1979, AN AIR NEW ZEALAND DC-10 ON A SIGHTSEEING FLIGHT OVER THE ANTARCTIC FLEW INTO THE SIDE OF A 12,000 FT VOLCANO NEAR SCOTT BASE .

THE AIRCRAFT HAD DESCENDED BELOW A 6,000 FT OVERCAST TO GIVE THE PASSENGERS A VIEW OF THE ICE-SHELF BELOW.

THE REVISED CO-ORDINATES WERE ENTERED BY THE CREW IMMEDIATELY BEFORE DEPARTURE, AND PUT THE DC-10 25 MILES TO THE SIDE OF THE CORRECT TRACK – UP MCMURDO SOUND AND DIRECTLY IN LINE WITH MOUNT EREBUS.

(e) the black hole phenomenon. 

A pilot can become seriously disorientated on a dark night where there is no horizon or outside visual/light reference on the ground. Under these conditions visual judgement of height above the ground becomes impossible due to a lack of depth perception.

This is known as the black hole phenomenon.

It is also common approaching an isolated airfield where there are few off-airfield lights to aid in orientation.

Most often it will be seen as an undershoot on finals.

The most effective defense is to disregard the deceptive visual information and trust the instruments. The altimeter, vertical speed indicator (VSI), and any available glideslope or VASI/PAPI systems are the only reliable sources of information in these conditions. DME / distance calculations can also ensure the 3 x profile is correct.


10.18.28 Describe conditions which can lead to the creation of a false horizon. 

Sloping layers of cloud by day, angled lines on the ground or areas of lights at night can sometimes present a pilot with a false horizon. This can be misleading and result in the pilot selecting false attitudes and receiving conflicting information.

           

A false horizon can be caused by:

• Flying over a layer of overcast cloud

• Flying between 2 layers of cloud

• Flying over snow with an overcast layer of cloud above (“white out”)

• Low flying/mountain flying with obstacles your obscuring view of the horizon

• Night flying when flying over featureless terrain and no ground lights and a dark sky, or with ground lights that are indistinguishable from a dark sky with stars, the edge of ground lights can appear to be the horizon


10.18.30 Explain the effect of a false horizon on visual perception. 

When experiencing a false horizon, a pilots tendency will be to set the aircrafts attitude to the false horizon rather than the real horizon. In cases of a potential false horizon, a pilot must trust their instruments.




10.18.32 Explain relative motion

Movement of an adjacent vehicle causes the occupants of a stationary one to think that they are moving. An aircraft taxing may think they have speed up of an adjacent aircraft that has been pushed backward.

Relative motion also makes it easier to spot an aircraft in the sky. Often an aircraft on a collision course with you may take the form of a small speck on your windscreen that gets bigger and bigger. Because of the lack of movement across your windscreen and aircraft on a collision course with you will be more difficult to spot than one that is not. A 747 can remain unseen until it fills your windscreen!


Any two aircraft on a collision course will maintain the same relative bearing whereas aircraft not on a collision course have changing relative bearings. In the latter case the relative movement is likely to attract the eyes, but the former may be missed for several reasons:

- If the closing aircraft is hidden by some structural obstructions, for example a windscreen pillar, it may never be seen

- The retinal size of the approaching aircraft increases with closing distance, but not in direct proportion to time-to-impact. The angular size of an incoming aircraft expands slowly until just before impact, when it expands very rapidly.

- The retina blind spot may hide any approaching aircraft.


10.18.34 Explain the effect of fog, haze, and/or dust on visual perception. 

The effect of air transparency (visibility) effects distance judgement. In clear air objects appear to be closer than they really are (on a clear day you can see forever). In fog or haze objects appear to be further away than they really are. 

Flying in haze creates the impression that the runway is farther away, inducing a tendency to appear shallow on the glide path and land long; Shallow fog (i.e., fog layer not exceeding 300 ft in thickness) results in a low obscuration but also in low horizontal visibility.

On a very clear day this can lead to the perception that an aircraft is high on the approach (overshooting) when in fact the approach may be correct/ leading a pilot to correct for an error which is not real but an illusion. The result may be an undershoot if the pilot corrects for the illusion of the overshoot. 

Flying in light rain, fog , haze, mist, smoke, or dust usually creates an illusion of being too low (it looks like you are further away from the runway than you actually are). The perception that an aircraft is low on the approach (undershooting) when in fact the approach may be correct is again due to an illusion. The result may be an overshoot if the pilot corrects for the illusion of the undershoot and a long landing.

Visual Illusions

When on top of a shallow fog layer, the ground (or airport and runway, if flying overhead) can be seen, but when entering the fog layer the forward and slant visibility usually are lost; Entering a fog layer also creates the perception of a pitch up, thus inducing a tendency to push over and place the aircraft below the desired glide path and in a steeper-than-desired attitude.

In light rain or moderate rain, the runway may also appear fuzzy because of rain halo effect, increasing the risk of not perceiving a vertical deviation or lateral deviation during the visual segment.

Pilots are prone to mind sets in conditions of low visibility (e.g. mist or cloud), or altered visibility (e.g. whiteout) or indirect visibility (e.g. using a radar screen).

A mind set is a strong mental picture of an expected situation. A mind set will affect a pilots PERCEPTION on a certain situation.

It is well known that in some situations if a pilot has a strong expectation of seeing something, when the expected situation arises the pilot will see what is expected, rather than what is actually there. Mindsets occur because visual images are capable of different interpretation ie when map reading or navigating.

The danger of a mindset is that when the actual situation occurs, the pilot may act on the mind set rather than on the actual circumstances.

Both pilots in the same cockpit can experience the same visual illusion simultaneously. Since they share the same external visual environment, illusions like the black hole effect, false horizon, or autokinesis can affect both pilots at the same time.

However, even if both experience the same illusion, their perception and reaction might differ due to differences in viewpoint, experience, and physiological responses. Despite this, the shared visual cues can produce a common deceptive perception for both pilots simultaneously. Breakdowns in scanning do not cause the illusions themselves; rather, scanning techniques help mitigate their effects.


10.18.36 Describe the optical characteristics of the windshield. 

Clean Windscreens

Factors impairing vision can be made worse if the pilot has a dirty or badly scratched windscreen to look through.

Always clean windows and windscreens inside and out.

WIPE VERTICALLY rather than horizontally before every flight.

Make sure that the cleaning agent and applicator will not scratch the windscreen.

Always remove jewelry and watches before cleaning any window


The effect of rain on the windshield. 

Rain on windshields creates a refraction and the perception of being too high, thus inducing a nose down correction that places the aircraft below the desired flight path


10.18.38 Explain the effect of sloping terrain on visual perception. 

The use of visual cues during landing. 

When learning to fly, a student pilot will become familiar with the shape and perspective of the runway.

This is particularly important on the final approach to landing, and pilots soon learn the correct approach perspective of the runway. They also learn how this changes when their approach is made too steeply, or lower than normal.

The ‘correct’ perspective that the student pilot has learnt to adopt can alter under various circumstances and can trick the unwary pilot.

To fly a 3 degree approach the visual angle (the angle between the horizon and the touchdown point on the runway) is 3 degrees (assuming a flat earth)



The slope of the terrain around a runway is also a potential source of illusions during approach and landing. It is difficult to maintain a glideslope visually when flying a runway approach over sloping terrain. 

If the terrain slopes up towards the runway we suffer the illusion of being higher than glideslope because the ground is further away than we expect. The pilot will then make a dangerously low approach


Be careful to read the wording in the exam question. In this example the text describes "Runway sloping down to the approach threshold". This is an upsloping runway!




10.18.40 Explain the effect of the following factors on visual perception during an approach: 

(a) steep/shallow approach angles; 

When a pilot used to landing on a relatively short runway makes an approach onto a LONGER RUNWAY, the runway will appear narrower than usual. The illusion will be that the aircraft is too high.

The pilot will descend to adopt the ‘usual’ perspective and make a dangerously LOW APPROACH.

When flying onto a SHORTER RUNWAY than the pilot is normally used to, the runway will appear wider than usual.
The illusion will be that the aircraft is too low.

The pilot will climb to adopt the ‘usual’ perspective and will make a HIGH APPROACH.



(b) length, width and texture of the runway; 

Runway Size

Runway size also plays a large part in the pilots perspective of the approach. A runway that is wider than usual will appear to be closer than it really is. This wide runway, because of the angle at which you view it peripherally in the final stages of the approach, will also cause an illusion of being to low, and you may flare and hold-off too high as a result.

Conversely, a runway that is narrower than usual will appear to be further away than it really is. This narrow runway may cause an illusion of being too high, and you may delay the flare and make contact with the runway earlier than expected.

Runway perspective - equal length but different widths




It is important to understand the variations in runway perspective and how to compensate correctly for them. Knowing what to expect is halfway to solving the problem.

You can use the dimensions and slope given in the VFG for an unfamiliar airfield in order to anticipate approach illusions and allow for them. Airport visual landing aids can be used to check you are on the correct approach path.

(c) the intensity of the approach lights. 

Approach and runway lighting:

The approach and runway lighting (including the touchdown zone lighting) affects the depth perception as a function of:

The lighting intensity;
The daytime or night time conditions; and,
The weather conditions.

Bright runway-lights create the impression of being closer to the runway (hence on a steeper glide path);

Low intensity lights create the impression of being farther away (hence on a shallower glide path);

A non-standard spacing of runway lights also modifies the pilot’s perception of the runway distance and glide path;

If runway lighting is partially visible (e.g., during the downwind leg or during the base leg of a visual or circling approach), the runway may appear being farther away or at a different angle (i.e., the intercept angle is perceived as smaller than actual).


Aviation Visual Illusions


The Effect Of ‘G’ Forces On Vision

Positive G forces will reduce the blood supply to the brain and therefore the oxygen supply will also be diminished.
The pilot will notice changes to their vision as follows:

1 . LOSS OF COLOUR (GREYING OUT)
2 . LOSS OF PERIPHERAL VISION (TUNNEL VISION )
3 . BLURRING OF VISION (LOSS OF VISUAL SHARPNESS)
4 . TOTAL LOSS OF VISION ( BLACKOUT)

Blackout will eventually occur. This is the total loss of vision due to the G forces. The pilot is NOT UNCONSCIOUS, just cannot see.
