10.66 Survival


The wreckage of Ansett NZ flight 703 lies on a barren hilltop after it crashed on approach to Palmerston North.



10.66.2 State the components of a pre-flight passenger briefing by a pilot with respect to aircraft safety features and equipment. 

Before taking any passengers on a flight whether private or commercial you are required to give a pre-flight passenger briefing with respect to the aircraft safety features and equipment. This will involve talking about:
  • LOCATION OF EXITS
  • LOCATION AND ACTIVATION OF ELT
  • LOCATION OF FIRST AID KIT
  • LOCATION AND USE OF FIRE EXTINGUISHER
  • SEAT BELTS
  • SMOKING
  • CELL PHONES
  • BRACE POSITION

10.66.4 Explain the basic steps in post-crash survivor management. 

One of the biggest potential killers after a crash is how you respond in the first minute. Freaking out won’t help you; gaining situational awareness will. Be aware that the impact and shock of the crash may be disorienting, but you need to pull yourself together and quickly assess your situation and approach the situation with the right attitude.

Panic renders otherwise-capable people unable to do something as simple as releasing a seatbelt. In the panic of the moment, people will forget how to operate the simple pull-release mechanisms and many crash victims are found still strapped into their seats.

If there is going to be a post-impact fire, it will most likely start very soon, or is already underway. If the hot engine starts a grass fire and the fuel tanks are breached, it only takes a few seconds before the entire plane can be engulfed in flames capable of melting aluminium. After you finish unbelting yourself, you may need to be reaching for the fire extinguisher.

Exit the aircraft. Assuming you aren’t precariously suspended from a tree, building or tower, you need to get yourself and your passengers out of and away from the plane. You may not have a great deal of time to do so.

While we value the heroics of those who selflessly put others first, it is important to do so wisely. Assess the situation before taking action that might put yourself or others at greater risk. Do you reach for the fire extinguisher or run away? Do you go back to a smouldering airplane to gather survival gear? The more rescue, first-responder or first-aid training you have, the more useful you will be assessing the situation and making the right call in an emergency. If you don’t have much training, simply remember one of the cardinal rules of rescue: Don’t create another victim by putting yourself at undue risk.

After extricating yourself and passengers from the aircraft, stage yourself in a safe location. From a safe location you can render first aid to stabilise injuries as best as you can. Once you have injuries stabilised, you need to do your part to help with the ‘search’ portion of search and rescue.

Good post-crash survivor management involves primarily securing the aircraft and evacuating, attending to passengers and any injured people. Following this attempt to contact authorities and activate ELT if it is not activated already.

STOP is a good acronym for Sit, Think, Observe, and Plan. In the immediate aftermath of the accident, you should take a few moments to sit and collect your thoughts. This is a good time to drink some water, as water will help to treat any post crash shock. You can then think about the situation and transition your situation from a crisis to the coping stage. Take this time to observe the area, inventory your survival equipment, and attend to any injuries. Now you are ready to make a plan for your survival.

Principles for survival

Experience has shown that in any survival situation, adherence to certain basic principles will increase a pilot’s chance of survival.

FIVE PROVEN PRINCIPLES FOR SURVIVAL ARE:

1. PROTECTION

Before any thought is given to medical treatment of injuries, it is important to first of all assess the accident site for potential hazards. Move away from the aircraft.

SUCH POTENTIAL HAZARDS INCLUDE:
  • FIRE
  • SMOKE AND LEAKING FUEL
  • OIL OR HYDRAULIC FLUID
  • BROKEN POWER WIRES
  • FALLEN TREES
  • WATER
  • PRECARIOUS AIRCRAFT POSITION
  • MOTOR VEHICLE TRAFFIC
Conduct any first aid that is possible.

Shelter has priority over food and water as exposure to the elements can cause death within hours. Protect yourself and passengers from hostile environment.



2 . LOCATION

As soon as possible, the downed pilot must carry out some measures to try and increase the chances of searching aircraft and others finding the crash location. If carried, activate Emergency Locator Transmitter (ELT). Clear away growth and set out ground signals, attract attention of rescuers using mirrors and flares. Use rocks or stones to create markers on ground, build fires. Prepare bonfires with available petrol or oil to create smoke during the day, fire at night.

3 . WATER

The human body is comprised of around 80 percent water and without it we perish. It is far more important than food.
The body survives for only days without water but can survive weeks or more without food. Some pilots will have a water bottle with them while others will find it nearby in a stream or river.

4 . FOOD

A supply of food is, in the end, essential for life although the body can survive in the starved state for days if not weeks.

Some food may be carried aboard the downed aircraft or by the pilot. Other food may have to be gathered from natural resources.

5 . MORALE - THE WILL TO SURVIVE 

Research has shown that the success or otherwise of humans in a survival situation depends on a number of factors including survival training and experience, self-awareness and self-confidence.

But outweighing all these factors in true-life survival situations appears to be a strong motivator called the will to survive. “I need to come out of this alive”. Strongly motivated humans have been known to survive for extended periods in the most trying and atrocious conditions despite circumstances in which they should theoretically have perished.


10.66.6 List the priorities of survival in order of importance. 

In any survival situation, there are priorities. These priorities are established based on how long a human can survive without each item. The list below is based on the "Rule of Three's": 
  1. Air  (3 minutes) 
  2. Shelter (3 hours) 
  3. Rest (30 hours) 
  4. Water (3 days) 
  5. Food (30 days)

10.66.8 Explain typical survival items that could be carried on a cross-country flight over bush clad and mountainous terrain. 

Survival equipment will be carried depending upon the nature of the terrain or water to be crossed. Some equipment is useful in all conditions (communication radios, ELT, compass, mobile phone) whereas others (like life rafts) are more specific.

In flight over bush or mountainous terrain additional, but discretionary, items may include:
  • FLARES
  • FLASHLIGHT
  • EXTRA CLOTHS
  • EMERGENCY RATIONS
  • KNIFE
  • ROPE
  • WHISTLE

10.66.10 Explain the process of hypothermia

Hypothermia isa potentially dangerous drop in body temperature, usually caused by prolonged exposure to cold temperatures. Hypothermia is defined as a body core temperature below 35.0 °C

In any survival situation precautions must be taken to prevent the onset of hypothermia. Cold water, wind or anything causing cold temperatures will cause the body to lose heat. Immersed in water at 20°C the body will lose a critical 4°C in about 2 hours. In water of 5°C this could occur in a few minutes.

Symptoms of hypothermia include skin that is cold to touch, slow pulse rate and shallow breathing. Elderly people and children are the most vulnerable.

To prevent hypothermia shelter the body, use warm blankets if available. Some food and drink will provide a source of energy to help internal warming.

If immersed in water the HELP AND HUDDLE POSITIONS will assist in reducing heat loss.
