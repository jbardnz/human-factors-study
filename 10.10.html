10.10.2  - Define Hypoxia

Hypoxia is a condition that exists when the available oxygen in the blood is inadequate for the demands of the body cells.

HYPOXIA is the RESULT of INSUFFICIENT OXYGEN PRESSURE. The OXYGEN is UNABLE to DIFFUSE THROUGH the LUNG LINING INTO the BLOOD SUPPLY to REACH the CELLS.

Hypoxia is caused when the oxygen breathed into the lungs has not got sufficient pressure to be able to diffuse through the semi-permeable lining of the lungs into the blood stream.

In flying this will most likely occur from a reduced atmospheric pressure at altitude.

The key to flying safely at altitude is to be able to: identify the flight condition in which you may become hypoxic, recognise your personal hypoxia symptoms, and to recover from hypoxia before you have gone beyond your ability or desire to help yourself.


34.10.4 State the partial pressure of oxygen both inside and outside the lungs at sea level.

The partial pressure of oxygen depends on several variables, but mainly on barometric pressure. Under physiological conditions, this relationship will be affected by any change in altitude.

At sea level atmospheric barometric pressure is about 760 mmHg, the partial pressure of oxygen is 21% of that:

Sea level partial pressure of oxygen = 159 mmHg

Barometric and Oxygen partial pressure vs altitude
                                              

Oxygen partial pressure in the lungs is lower than the atmospheric O2 partial pressure for two reasons.
  • Firstly, as the air enters the lungs, it is humidified by the upper airway and thus the partial pressure of water vapour (47 mmHg) reduces the oxygen partial pressure to about 150 mmHg.
  • The rest of the difference is due to the continual uptake of oxygen by the pulmonary capillaries, and the continual diffusion of CO2 out of the capillaries into the alveoli.

At sea level the partial pressure of oxygen in the lungs is 104 mmHg

The partial pressure of oxygen in alveolar air is about 104 mm Hg, whereas the partial pressure of oxygenated blood in pulmonary veins is about 100 mm Hg - this allows oxygen molecules to transfer across the lung wall.

Sea level pressure is 101 kPa (1013 hPa), Partial pressures of oxygen is 21 kPa outside the lungs and 14 kPa in the lungs.



10.10.6 Explain the mechanical effect of the partial pressure of oxygen on oxygen transfer in the lungs.

Inside the lung air sacs, oxygen moves across paper-thin walls to tiny blood vessels called capillaries and into your blood. 

The walls of the alveoli share a membrane with the capillaries. That's how close they are. This lets oxygen and carbon dioxide diffuse, or move freely, between the respiratory system and the bloodstream.

A protein called haemoglobin in the red blood cells then carries the oxygen around your body. At the same time, carbon dioxide that is dissolved in the blood comes out of the capillaries back into the air sacs, ready to be breathed out.

Lung air sacs



During inhalation, each alveoli receives air that contains various gases: nitrogen (almost 80%), oxygen (almost 20%) and other gases including 0.04% carbon dioxide. External gaseous exchange then takes place, using the principle of diffusion:

  • Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli into the pulmonary capillaries because there is a high concentration of oxygen in the lungs and a low concentration in the blood;
  • Carbon dioxide diffuses from the pulmonary capillaries into the alveoli because there is a high concentration of carbon dioxide in the blood and a low concentration in the lungs;
  • Nitrogen diffuses both ways.
Arterial oxygen levels (the rate of diffusion) is determined by inspired oxygen concentration and the pressure difference across the lung membrane. 

The rate of diffusion of oxygen is proportional to its partial pressure within the total gas mixture.





10.10.8 Explain the causes of hypoxia.

Hypoxia has several causal factors, including an insufficient supply of oxygen, inadequate transportation of oxygen, or the inability of the body tissues to use oxygen. The forms of hypoxia are based on their causes:

Hypoxic Hypoxia:

Hypoxic hypoxia results from insufficient oxygen available to the body as a whole. The reduction in partial pressure of oxygen at high altitude can cause a pilot to experience this type of hypoxia. As an unpressurized aircraft ascends during flight, the percentage of atmospheric oxygen remains constant, but a reduced number of oxygen molecules enter the lungs and pass between the membranes within the respiratory system.

Hypemic Hypoxia:

Hypemic hypoxia occurs when the blood cannot take up sufficient oxygen. Causes of this form of hypoxia include reduced blood volume from severe bleeding or certain blood diseases, such as anemia. Carbon monoxide poisoning causes this type of hypoxia. Hemoglobin, the blood molecule that transports oxygen, becomes chemically unable to bind oxygen molecules if exposed to carbon monoxide. Hypemic hypoxia can also occur after a blood donation. While blood volume normalises quickly following a donation, restoring the lost hemoglobin can take several weeks. Although the effects of the blood loss seem slight at ground level, blood donation can create a flight risk during the recovery period.

Stagnant Hypoxia:

Stagnant hypoxia or ischemia results when the oxygen-rich blood in the lungs does not move to the tissues that need it. An arm or leg going to sleep because of restricted blood flow is one form of stagnant hypoxia. This kind of hypoxia can also result from shock, the heart failing to pump blood effectively, or a constricted artery. During flight, excessive G forces can cause stagnant hypoxia. Cold temperatures can also reduce circulation and decrease blood supply to extremities.

Histotoxic Hypoxia:

When histotoxic hypoxia occurs, the body transports enough oxygen to the cells, but they cannot make use of it. This impairment of respiration at the cellular level may result from alcohol consumption, exposure to certain drugs or narcotics, or exposure to poisons.

The most common causes of hypoxia in aviation are: flying, non-pressurised aircraft above 10,000 ft without supplemental oxygen, rapid decompression during flight, pressurisation system malfunction, pilot error, oxygen system malfunction (failure of the mask or regulator), or carbon monoxide poisoning.


Pressurisation panel - packs off, no duct pressure, outflow valve fully open - unpressurised cabin



If the pilot is fit and free from disease and illness is sitting quietly at the controls in a warm cabin or cockpit he or she will have a better tolerance to hypoxia (but will eventually become unconscious unless oxygen is obtained).

Tolerance to hypoxia will be considerably shortened, and hypoxia can be expected to occur below 10,000 ft AMSL if the pilot:
  • HAS A COLD OR CHEST INFECTION, OR IS ILL
  • HAS A HANGOVER
  • HAS OTHER DRUG EFFECTS
  • MOVES QUICKLY ABOUT THE AIRCRAFT
  • IS SUBJECTED TO COLD TEMPERATURES (LIVER USES OXYGEN TO WARM THE BODY)
  • IS SUBJECTED TO STRONG G FORCES
  • IS ANAEMIC
  • IS FATIGUED
  • IS A SMOKER
  • CARBON MONOXIDE

The primary physiological and behavioural consequences of hypoxia for flight crew and passengers.

Without enough oxygen, the body will lose consciousness.

The initial effects of hypoxia are not unlike alcohol. A feeling of carefree euphoria is sometimes the first signal that something’s wrong. Other signs include blue fingernails or lips, headache, delayed response times, or impaired judgment.

As the body’s tissues become more and more starved for oxygen, numbness, tingling fingers and toes, drowsiness, and lightheadedness will follow. The field of vision will narrow, and unconsciousness follows.

As the body gets less oxygen, hypoxia progresses through four stages.

The first stage, the Indifferent Stage, really only affects one part of the body–the eyes. The process of dark adaptation is extremely oxygen intensive. As a result, eyesight is severely limited at night if there is low oxygen available. This is the reason that the FAA recommends supplemental oxygen above 5,000 feet MSL at night.

The second stage is the Compensatory Stage. During this phase, the body automatically attempts to make up for the lack of oxygen by increasing the breathing rate or taking deeper breaths.

Next comes the Disturbance Stage, where the body can no longer make up for the lack of oxygen, and signs and symptoms listed above can become evident.

The final stage of hypoxia is the Critical Stage, where consciousness is lost, and death follows shortly after that.

Hearing is particularly resistant to hypoxia which is one reason many crew members have been saved from near tragedy. Radio is the best way to communicate with a pilot suffering from acute hypoxia. Simple orders from pilots in other planes, controllers, or flight engineers have been used to guide pilots to safety.



10.10.10 Describe the common symptoms of hypoxia.

Symptoms of hypoxia:

THE ONSET CAN BE SUBTLE SO THE SUFFERER MAY NOT BE AWARE OF IT.

The most complex parts of the brain use the most oxygen so will be affected earliest by hypoxia. They are responsible for functions such as personality, reasoning, memory, judgement, decision making, and vision.

The first symptoms of hypoxia can include euphoria and a carefree feeling. With increased oxygen deprivation, the extremities become less responsive and flying becomes less coordinated.

HYPOXIA WILL NOT AFFECT ALL PILOTS IN THE SAME WAY

  • Cyanosis (blue fingernails and lips).
  • Headache
  • Decreased response to stimuli and increased reaction time,
  • Poor or Impaired judgment
  • Euphoria
  • Visual impairment - tunnel vision, reduced colour vision
  • Drowsiness
  • Lightheaded or dizzy sensation
  • Tingling in fingers and toes
  • Numbness
  • Mental fixation on unimportant tasks

As hypoxia worsens, the pilot's field of vision begins to narrow, and instrument interpretation can become difficult. Even with all these symptoms, the effects of hypoxia can give a pilot a false sense of security and be deceived into believing everything is normal.



34.10.12 Explain the reasons hypoxia symptoms are difficult to detect.

One of the biggest challenges in dealing with hypoxia during flight is that the effects are not always evident at first. This, coupled with the fact that its effects are cumulative, makes it difficult to recognise.



10.10.14 Explain the relationship between hypoxic onset and both vision and cognitive performance.

The symptoms of brain hypoxia are:
  • temporary memory loss.
  • reduced ability to move your body.
  • difficulty paying attention.
  • difficulty making sound decisions.
The visual system is sensitive to its oxygen supply at multiple levels.

Cone photoreceptors, which enable color vision, consume more energy than rods, as rods do not saturate in bright light.  In total, visual processing ranks as one of the highest energy- and oxygen-demanding systems of the brain.

Due to these demands, it is unsurprising that exposure to hypoxic conditions results in changes to the perception of light intensity and impairs colour vision

Some of the most commonly reported symptoms of hypoxia include graying, tunnel vision, and blurry vision


34.10.16 Describe how hypoxia can be prevented.

There are two methods for preventing hypoxia. 

One method involves increasing barometric pressure to a minimal value such that the concentration of oxygen is sufficient to prevent hypoxia from occurring. This is generally done by pressurising the aircraft cabin. 

The other method is to increase the breathable oxygen in a system through the use of an O2 device. This is usually accomplished by having a pilot (or passengers and crew) wear an oxygen mask.

It is also possible to combine the methods to provide greater assurance that hypoxia is prevented.

The most effective way to prevent hypoxia is through education and experience. When pilots are trained in the proper use and care of their pressurisation systems and supplemental oxygen equipment, and are aware of their personal hypoxia signs and symptoms, they are safer.

The main factors influencing variation in hypoxia onset (tolerance) between individuals.

Factors that will place a pilot’s time of useful consciousness in the lower (more dangerous) end of the range are those that place extra demands on the body for oxygen.

These include illness, physical activity, and a cold body temperature.
  • ILLNESS – especially colds and flu. The respiration system is not working at its full capacity.
  • PHYSICAL ACTIVITY – the body is already breathing faster, to provide cells with the required oxygen. There is less residual oxygen to be used for respiration.
  • COLD BODY TEMPERATURE – the liver will be using a lot more oxygen than usual in trying to heat up the rest of the body. Less oxygen again remains for respiration.
It is impossible to predict exactly when, where, or how hypoxic reactions will occur in an individual pilot. The appearance and severity of the signs and symptoms are aggravated by several factors; rate of ascent, time spent at altitude, physical activity at altitude, fatigue, self-imposed stress, extreme ambient temperature, and individual physiological fitness.

Hypoxia Facts

1. A healthy pilot should not be affected significantly by hypoxia below 10,000ft amsl.
2. If a pilot is unwell with a chest infection it is possible to suffer effects of hypoxia below 10,000 ft.
3. Carbon monoxide gas and high G loading can also produce hypoxia at any altitude.
4. The most complex parts of the brain use the most amount of oxygen so will be affected earliest by hypoxia.
5. They are responsible for functions such as personality, reasoning, memory, judgement, decision-making, and vision.
6. Hypoxia can be recognised by progressive symptoms.
7. The onset of hypoxia can be subtle so the sufferer may not be aware of it.
8. Hypoxia will not affect all pilots in the same way.


46.10.18 State the factors that affect the likelihood of suffering from hypoxia.

If the body’s red blood cells are unable to carry oxygen to the organs in medicine, it is caused by severe blood loss or anemia. A possible cause of this in aviation is carbon monoxide poisoning.

Drugs and alcohol can cause the organs not to use sufficient oxygen. While the effects may be negligible at the surface, at altitude they may be significantly amplified.

It’s also possible for the blood to not flow enough to feed the organs all of the oxygen they need. On the ground, this could be caused by cardiovascular disease. In flight, this is commonly caused by G-forces.

As an aircraft pulls more G’s, the heart and cardiovascular system must work harder to keep blood flowing to the head and brain. The increased pull of gravity will make it harder and harder to do.

Eventually, this will lead to tunnel vision as the eyes receive insufficient oxygen, and then grey out and blackout.

Smoking introduces carbon monoxide, which binds to haemoglobin more strongly than oxygen, reducing oxygen delivery. Fatigue further reduces efficiency of oxygen utilisation.


46.10.20 Describe how hypoxia can be treated.

Treatment Of Hypoxia

A person suffering from hypoxia must have the pressure of oxygen that they are breathing increased so the cells can again be supplied with oxygen to complete the respiration process. This can be done by:
  • ADMINISTERING OXYGEN FROM AN OXYGEN SUPPLY SOURCE; and/or
  • DESCENDING THE AIRCRAFT (or the aircraft cabin) TO 10,000 FT AMSL OR BELOW.

If hypoxia is suspected in yourself or others on board an aircraft, follow these simple steps:

  • Administer supplemental oxygen (don your oxygen mask)
  • Check your equipment for proper operation
  • Ensure the regulator is turned on
  • Check the flow indicator (this will tell you that something is coming to the mask)
  • Ensure that all oxygen equipment connections are secure
  • Monitor your breathing rate and depth (intentionally slow your breathing to prevent hyperventilation, use the flow indicator to help you monitor respiration)
  • If safe, descend to an altitude (below 10,000 feet) where supplemental oxygen is no longer required
