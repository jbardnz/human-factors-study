10.22 Spacial Orientation



10.22.2 Define spatial orientation. 

SPATIAL ORIENTATION is basically the ability to know which way is up. The  Psychology Definition of SPATIAL ORIENTATION is Being able to change location in space in relation to objects we can see.

It defines our natural ability to maintain our body orientation and/or posture in relation to the surrounding environment (physical space) at rest and during motion. Genetically speaking, humans are designed to maintain spatial orientation on the ground. The three-dimensional environment of flight is unfamiliar to the human body, creating sensory conflicts and illusions that make spatial orientation difficult, and sometimes impossible to achieve. Statistics show that between 5 to 10% of all general aviation accidents can be attributed to spatial disorientation, 90% of which are fatal.


10.22.4 Define disorientation. 

Spatial disorientation is the inability of a person to determine his/her true body position, motion, and altitude relative to the earth or the surroundings.

Pilots can easily become disorientated while flying during periods of challenging visibility (in cloud or on instruments), since vision is the dominant sense for orientation. Without a visual horizon, disorientation can be common, as a result of sensory illusions of the balance organs.

Sensory input

Spatial disorientation, if not corrected, can lead to both loss of control and controlled flight into terrain. The possibility of becoming spatially disorientated is hard-wired into all humans. In fact, it is the proper functioning of our spatial orientation system, which provides the illusion; and because this is a system we have learnt to trust, it is particularly difficult for some people, in some circumstances, to accept that their orientation isn’t what it appears to be. Despite the capability, accuracy, reliability and flexibility of modern flight displays and instrumentation, pilots can still find themselves questioning what the aircraft is telling them, because the “seat of their pants” or “gut feeling” is saying something else. No one is immune.


10.22.6 Outline the anatomy of the motion, orientation and gravitational sensory organs, including: (a) the semi-circular canals; (b) vestibular sac/tubes. 

10.22.8 Outline the physiology of the motion, orientation and gravitational sensory organs, including: (a) the semi-circular canals (b) vestibular sac/tubes.

There are three systems that provide the body with orientation information.

The brain uses information from all three systems to determine body orientation and movement.
  • VISION
  • VESTIBULAR APPARATUS
  • PROPRIOCEPTORS
Vestibular Apparatus (Balance Organs)

The balance organs are very small and are located in the inner ear. There is one set per ear.

Each balance organ has two parts: SEMICIRCULAR CANALS and an OTOLITH ORGAN.


Semi Circular Canals

These are three fluid-filled canals mounted at right angles to each other. They sense ANGULAR ACCELERATION in three different planes, similar to those of an aircraft.


They sense individual accelerations, and combinations of these:
  • PITCHING UP OR DOWN
  • ROLLING LEFT OR RIGHT
  • TURNING OF THE HEAD (YAWING) LEFT OR RIGHT
Inside these canals is a watertight swing-door that is deflected by movement of the fluid.

This stimulates nerves that generate information for the brain on head movements.

Once the angular acceleration has stopped e.g. in a steady-rate turn, the canals will no longer register any change.
This can lead to disorientation problems, including the leans.

Although the semi-circular canals are quite sensitive they will not pick up very small accelerations. If the accelerating force is too low, it will not be picked up and this too can lead to problems of orientation.

Semicircular Canals

10.22.10 Explain the interconnection between the visual and kinaesthetic senses in maintaining accurate spatial orientation. 

Kinaesthetic sense is the sense of body position and movement of body parts relative to each other. The brain uses prior experience to predict the visual consequences of motor commands. If an action is performed without its typical visual result (e.g., in total darkness), individuals can still experience a "visual sensation of motion" driven purely by kinaesthetic signals and predictive brain activity. It is sometimes called muscle memory.

The visual and kinaesthetic senses are deeply interconnected, working seamlessly together through a process called visual-motor integration to provide a coherent sense of the body's position and movement in space and to enable coordinated action.

Kinaesthetic senses in aviation, often referred to as the "seat of the pants" feeling, provide a sense of body position and motion based on signals from the skin, muscles, and joints. However, these senses are easily fooled during flight, leading to spatial disorientation, especially in low-visibility conditions where the pilot cannot rely on visual cues. This can lead to dangerous situations like unrecognised turns or dives because the body's physical sensations conflict with the actual state of the aircraft.

Vision

Vision plays a large part in orientation. It accounts for at least 80 PERCENT of body orientation information received by the brain.

From their first flight, pilots are taught to fly by visually comparing the aircraft’s attitude with the earth’s horizon.
Example, for straight and level flight:

If the attitude changes so that the nose of the
aircraft is too high above the horizon, the aircraft
will climb.



Similarly, if the attitude changes so that the nose of the aircraft is too low below the horizon, the aircraft will descend.

Vision is the most powerful information system feeding the brain for orientation, but is vulnerable to visual illusions.


Conflicting Information To The Brain

Moving around on the earth’s surface, no great problem is usually encountered with orientation so long as all three systems are working correctly and are not being subjected to confusing or conflicting input information.

The flying environment provides a high potential for conflict of orientation information to the brain. Any change in aircraft attitudes, outside visibility, G-forces and/or speed can tax the human orientation system to its limit. The pilot will become disorientated if the limit is exceeded.

Pilots rely heavily on the earth’s horizon for orientation. If the horizon is obscured or distorted, the pilot will lose a major reference point for orientation. This will then force the pilot to rely on other senses for orientation. ‘Flying by the seat of your pants’ is usually fatal.


10.22.12 Explain the bodies limitations in maintaining spatial orientation when vision is adversely affected. 

Proprioceptors (Nerve Receptors)

This is a network of nerve receptors around the body comprising pressure-area receptors, muscle-stretch receptors and limb-position receptors.

The pressure-area receptors are sensitive to pressure over various parts of the body, in particular the pressure created by the force of gravity. The limb-position and muscle-stretch receptors allow the brain to know where body limbs are positioned in space even with the eyes closed.

The pressure-area sensors are the most important as they are the body’s G-meter and will indicate to the pilot whether the aircraft is accelerating in positive or negative G modes.

But the pressure-area sensors are limited in their usefulness. The same G force can be experienced in many different manoeuvres, such as a loop, dive, or a very steep turn. The sensation that is felt will be the same in all of these manoeuvres. Pilot sensation will also be the same in inverted and normal flight.

Graveyard Spiral


If the pilot uses these sensors alone disorientation can easily occur.


10.22.14 Describe and explain the effects of the following spatial illusions: 

(a) the leans and sub-threshold stimulation; 

The Leans

THE LEANS is a strong sensation felt by the pilot of being in one attitude when all the aircraft flight instruments are indicating that the aircraft is on a different attitude. For example…

The aircraft is banked in a rate one turn to the right. The turn is maintained at the correct angle of bank for one minute and then rolls back wings level. Simple.

BUT what the pilot thinks is happening…

The pilot rolls to the right into a rate one turn using just the instruments. The semicircular canals indicate to the pilot the turn to the right. As the turn becomes constant after a short time the turning acceleration and sensation become steadily less until there is no sensation of turning.


When the pilot rolls back to wings level, he now experiences a strong sensation of turning to the left! This can be particularly dangerous. Believe your instruments and not body sensation.


SUB-THRESHOLD STIMULATION is similar to the leans.

Example…

A pilot who is flying solely on instruments very slowly and unwittingly rolls off his heading; for example to the left. The pilot suddenly realises this and quickly rolls back to wings level using the instruments only.

Because the semicircular canals do not pick up slow movement they give the pilot no sensation of turning. When the pilot quickly rolls back to wings level he will feel a strong sensation of turning to the right. The pilot has the leans again.



(b) somatogravic illusion; 

Otolith Organs

This is located adjacent to the semi-circular canals. It consists of a tiny movement-sensitive, jelly-like mass sitting on top of a group of vertical hairs. The Otolith senses both head/body tilt and linear acceleration.

Otolith hairs sense movement

Somatogravic Illusion

During rapid acceleration take offs or overshoots (a go-around) another form of disorientation can occur. This is called SOMATOGRAVIC ILLUSION.

If the aircraft is accelerating quickly (e.g. takeoff, overshoot) the pilot gets a strong sensation that the aircraft is CLIMBING STEEPLY.


If the pilot reacts to the illusion (instead of believing the instruments) he will dive the aircraft into the ground!

The Otolith organ confuses acceleration with tilting your head backwards. If a pilot is flying only on instruments with no visual horizon dangerous sensory illusions can occur.


The inadvertent somatogravic effect can occur in all aircraft but is a specific problem in helicopters. A helicopter has no source of force in the longitudinal direction of the fuselage. In order to accelerate in the line of flight, a helicopter has to pitch down so that a component of the lift force generated by the rotor acts in a forward direction. 

The illusory sense of pitch-up that accompanies forward acceleration almost exactly compensates the actual pitch-down required to achieve that acceleration, and in consequence, the aircraft continues to feel level. Likewise, in order to reduce airspeed, the helicopter pilot has to put the aircraft into a nose-up attitude so that a component of lift acts in a backward direction. The pitch-down effect of deceleration negates the actual pitch-up. 

An alternative way to explain this effect is to recognise that, neglecting the tail rotor, the only source of aerodynamic force is the force exerted by the main rotor. Its predominantly upward pull creates a sense of gravity within the aircraft that remains vertically downward with respect to the aircraft, whatever its attitude, relative to the true vertical. It is true that to initiate a change of attitude in pitch or roll, the pilot has aerodynamically to tilt the rotor with respect to the fuselage, but this is only transient. The fuselage soon follows the alignment of the rotor disc. 

Also, it is important to recognise that the somatogravic effect is not evidence of shortcomings in the gravity sensors within the inner ear; accelerometers would give the same indication.

(c) somatogyral illusion; 
SOMATOGYRAL ILLUSION occurs during a prolonged turning manoeuvre at a constant angular speed, the middle ear’s semicircular canals can only detect the start and end of the angular motion. They do not detect the steady state in between. When the steady state is achieved, the time taken for the sensation of turning to drop below the threshold level is between 10 and 20 seconds.

However, on recovery from the angular motion, the fluid in the canals is accelerated from its steady state as the canals themselves stop moving with respect to the fluid. If this exceeds the threshold level, the action is perceived as the start of rotation in the opposite direction to the original motion

Graveyard Spin

“The Graveyard Spin” is a term used to describe the result of an incorrect recovery from a spin caused by the Somatogyral Illusion and which may lead to disaster.

During a prolonged spin, the fluid(endolymph) within the semicircular canals settles, thereby allowing the sensing hairs to erect. When a recovery from the spin is initiated (as in the case of a prolonged turn), the hairs of semicircular canals again are moved, giving the pilot a strong sensation of entering in a spin in the opposite direction. If he/she succumbs and reacts to this incorrect information, the pilot will re-enter the spin in the original direction


(d) cross coupled turning (coriolis effect); 

If you simultaneously induce motion in two semicircular canals, e.g. by moving your head in pitch which the aircraft is rolling you will trigger a response known as the CORIOLIS EFFECT.

Lets say you are about to roll into a turn to the right and having just dropped your map, you have bent over to retrieve it. Were you to execute the roll at the same time as raising your head to the upright, you would induce motion in two semicircular canals and undergo cross couple turning (coriolis effect).


This is a violent form of vertigo that is very real. To experience the effects find a kids playground with one of those ‘rotating disc’ devices. Start rotating and hold on to the rail firmly, and gently lower your head to your chest. Then, a little less gently – but not to rapidly raise it. There will be wild reflex muscular spasms accompanied by powerful nausea. Something you do not want to experience while in control of an aircraft.

(e) pressure vertigo. 

Another form of vertigo is known as PRESSURE VERTIGO and can result from the effect on the balance apparatus following collapse of the eardrum due to blocked Eustachian Tubes. This could occur with a cold, when pressure increases outside the ear while the aircraft is descending at a high rate or from high altitude. It is due to unequal changes in middle ear pressures and is most often mild.

The SEMI-CIRCULAR CANALS and OTOLITH ORGANS Both Cause STRONG SENSORY ILLUSIONS !

10.22.16 Explain how disorientation can be prevented. 

Pilots will be able to avoid the serious consequences of disorientation while flying on instruments if they have a good understanding of the various types of disorientation and their effects, recognise flight and weather situations where disorientation is likely to occur, and anticipate any loss of orientation by starting an instrument scan early.

Manoeuvre the aircraft smoothly, relax, make small and slow head movements. If disorientation does occur – believe the instruments and not the body sensors.

Build up experience in controlling the aircraft in an environment of conflicting orientation cues - practice instrument go-arounds in the aircraft

If intending to fly at night, maintain night-flight currency. Include cross country and local operations at different airports.

Prioritise the workload. First fly the aircraft, then consider other things

Loss Of Control

Most cases of disorientation occur in conditions of poor visibility.

If a pilot presses on in bad weather, if the pilot does not transfer primary orientation to instruments straight away, total loss of control will occur usually in less than one minute.

Get Onto Instruments Early

If the pilot can see, or knows in advance, that the aircraft is about to enter cloud or that the horizon is about to disappear from view it is important that a transition to flying fully on instruments is made before instrument flight conditions are encountered.

Believe The Instruments

The message of these sensory illusions is clear. Pilots must be aware that during instrument flying the body’s balance organs can cause sensory illusions. Under these circumstances the pilots must believe the instruments – or run a risk of losing control !

Instrument Flying

Instrument flying refers to flight by reference to the aircraft’s flight instruments ONLY, without reference to the earth’s horizon or any other ground features outside the aircraft.

Not one flight instrument can give the overall aircraft flight situation. Nor can the pilot read and interpret more than one instrument at a time. Instrument flying therefore requires the use of several flight instruments, which the pilot needs
to scan in a regular and repetitive way.

Lapses in this scan or breaks in the pilot’s concentration can lead to loss of control of the aircraft or losing your mental picture of the aircraft’s position on the flight, therefore leading to accidents.
