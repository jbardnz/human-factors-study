10.30 Fitness to Fly


As pilots we all undergo a medical assessment. As we age, the checks become more comprehensive and more frequent. A medical is issued to a pilot by a certified medical examiner. Your license is not valid if you do not have a current medical. It is the responsibility of the pilot to keep their medical up to date and book in for their renewal when required.




10.30.2 Describe the term fitness to fly.

A pilot needs to be fit both mentally and physically to withstand the considerable and varying demands that can arise within the flying environment.

The pilot needs to be free from any disease, disability, illness or drug effect that could act suddenly or subtly to interfere with the ability to operate the aircraft.

A pilot needs to be capable of knowing when he or she is fit to fly and when he or she is not, and to develop an understanding of the factors that can affect such fitness.

Not all doctors have a working knowledge of the aviation environment and its effects on the human body.
Each pilot needs access to an approved aviation medicine examiner (AME).

When a pilot becomes ill or has a serious accident or needs surgery, the pilot must tell the aviation medical assessor.
The doctor will then assess their fitness to continue flying.

In cases where the pilot is designated not fit for a period, the doctor must re-assess the pilot as he or she becomes well again for a return to flying.

If a pilot is unable to meet the requirements for the medical certificate or unable to operate the aircraft in a safe manner then he/she is unfit to fly.


10.30.4 Explain the responsibilities of pilots towards medical fitness for flight.

The Civil Aviation Authority requires pilots in New Zealand to have a medical performed by a CAA designated Medical Examiner. It is important to remember that this is a regulatory job being done on behalf of a Government agency, not a job being done by a doctor for their patient. As a Designated Medical Examiner (DME) you are designated by CAA to act for them, on their behalf, for a period of generally 5 years.

On 01 April 2002 an amendment to the Civil Aviation Act, 1990 (the Act) established a new medical certification system. At the core of this system are Medical Examiners (MEs) designated by the Director of Civil Aviation (the Director) by being issued a certificate. This ME designation certificate is an Aviation Document, the privileges of which enable the ME to conduct examinations for a specified class of aviation medical certificates. These are described in the Civil Aviation Rules (the rules).

The rules are made by the Minister of Transport under the Civil Aviation Act. The medical standards are prescribed in Civil Aviation Rules (CAR) Part 67. General Directions (GDs) are issued by the Director under section 27G of the Act. They are items of legislation and are mandatory. They provide directions to MEs in relation to the conduct of medical examinations, and other requirements.

A grade 1 medical examiner may conduct medical examinations for the purpose of issuing any class of medical certificate, however a grade 2 examiner may only issue class 2 medicals.

Medical examinations of pilots determine their health status and potential risk to aviation safety.

Your license is not valid if you do not have a current medical.

10.30.6 Identify symptoms and circumstances that would lead you to consult your aviation medical examiner prior to further flight.

Certain medical incidents may require an individual to seek medical advice from their approved aviation medical doctor.

When a pilot has a minor illness, they may have difficulty deciding whether or not he or she is fit for flying.

If a pilot has significant difficulty in deciding whether he or she is fit to fly then an aviation medical assessor should be contacted for advice.

A minor illness can be described as any health problem that is troublesome, perhaps intermittently but is not severe enough to require hospitalisation or to stay at home in bed.

Minor illnesses usually come on quite quickly and often resolve just as quickly but they are not normal states of health.

Examples Of A Minor Illness Are:
  • A COUGH
  • A SORE THROAT
  • A COLD
  • A TOUCH OF THE FLU
  • MILD BRONCHITIS
  • HEADACHE
  • DIARRHEA
  • HAY FEVER
  • MUSCLE STRAIN
  • MENSTRUAL PROBLEMS
Most minor illnesses do not require heavy medication or drugs. Many require no medication at all and can be overcome quickly be simple non-drug measures such as rest and increased intake of clear fluids.

Pilots should not seek over the counter medicines from the local pharmacy or drug store and they should not use homeopathic drugs for any illness. Such medicines contain drugs that can have an adverse effect on the body, in particular the brain.

A good reason for pilots avoiding over the counter medicines is that most are often not a cure but treat only the symptoms, usually temporarily. If at any stage there is any doubt about a pilot’s ability to fly safely the message is clear – the pilot should not fly.

10.30.8 Describe the IMSAFE method of assessing fitness for flight.

Am I Fit To Fly?

The pilot always has to ask the simple question before each and every flight: Am I fit to fly?

Remember that any flight has the potential to become very demanding, often without warning.

A useful pneumonic developed by the FAA to jog the pilots’ memories before each flight is IM SAFE.


Only the pilot can answer this question. If there is significant doubt in the pilot’s mind then he or she should not fly.


10.30.10 Describe the problems associated with pregnancy and flying.

Flying While Pregnant

There is no physiological reason why a mother-to-be should not fly while pregnant, assuming she and her baby remain in good health.

Amnesia, nausea, tiredness or symptoms of low blood pressure (all common in pregnancy) are NOT COMPATIBLE with flying as a pilot.

I’m pregnant! Do I have to notify CAA? 

Yes, you are required to notify CAA as soon as you know or believe you are pregnant. Every pilot has an obligation, under Schedule 2, clause 8 of the Civil Aviation Act 2023, to report any change in their medical condition, or the existence of any previously unknown medical condition, that may interfere with flight safety. Pregnancy is certainly a medical condition that could interfere with flight safety and so must be reported. 

Can I keep flying? 

The early and late periods of pregnancy are associated with increased risk of potentially incapacitating complications such as miscarriage and premature labour. During late pregnancy the changes in the shape and size of your body also have the greatest potential to interfere with your movement and agility.

For these reasons flying status is usually retained during the low-risk middle period of an uncomplicated pregnancy (with a multi-crew restriction for air operations involving the carriage of passengers), but not prior to the end of the 12th week or after the end of the 28th week. Depending on the nature of any problems this window of time is likely to be reduced in the case of pregnancy complications.

Generally, class 1 medical certificate holders are precluded from flying, in any capacity, prior to the end of the 12th week and after the end of the 28th week. Class 2 certificate holders are usually precluded from flying in any capacity prior to the end of the 12th week, required to fly with a pilot during 28-32 weeks, and to not fly after 32 weeks


10.30.12 Describe the following factors, including their effects on pilot performance and methods by which they may be minimised/managed:

(a) arterial disease;

Damaging and hardening of the arteries causes coronary artery disease.

Plaque is a sticky substance made of cholesterol, fats, calcium, and other substances found in the blood. The buildup of this plaque, called atherosclerosis, occurs on the inner walls of your arteries, not on the red blood cells themselves.

This leads to a thickening of the artery wall and thereby a gradual reduction of the inside diameter.

The primary physiological problem caused by the buildup of plaque on the inner walls of your arteries is Atherosclerosis, which hardens and narrows the arteries.



A number of risk factors have been identified to show who is likely to have a heart attack and who is not.

The more risk factors a person has the greater their chance of having a heart attack.

The risk factors for coronary artery disease are:
  • A POSITIVE FAMILY HISTORY OF HEART DISEAS E
  • HIGH BLOOD SUGAR LEVEL (DIABETES )
  • SMOKING
  • ELEVATED BLOOD PRESSURE LEVELS (HYPERTENSION)
  • ELEVATED BLOOD CHOLESTEROL LEVEL
  • LACK OF EXERCISE
  • EXCESSIVE BODY WEIGHT (OBESITY)
  • STRESS
All of these except family history can be altered or eliminated.

Pilots who suffer a heart attack are grounded immediately.

Half will lose their licence permanently and the rest will take years to regain a medical certificate.

(b) blood pressure;

Your heart beats about 70 times a minute, and with each beat blood is pumped from the heart into the arteries.

As this happens the pressure inside the arteries goes up, until the end of that heart beat. The peak level of pressure is called the SYSTOLIC PRESSURE.

Then the heart relaxes and begins filling with blood for the next beat. While this is happening the pressure in the arteries starts to fall and reaches a minimum level just before the next beat. This is called the DIASTOLIC PRESSURE.

A NORMAL BLOOD PRESSURE IS 120/80 MM HG. The number 120 refers to the systolic pressure, and 80 to the diastolic pressure. The acceptable average range for peak pressure is 150-100 mm Hg, and for resting pressure is 95- 60 mm Hg.

High Blood Pressure (HYPERTENSION)

High blood pressure simply refers to an increased tension or pressure in the arteries.

High blood pressure damages arteries and is one of the prime causes of hardened arteries.

IT IS POSSIBLE TO REDUCE RAISED BLOOD PRESSURE.

The best method of doing so is to make life style alterations such as increasing physical fitness, altering diet, reducing coffee intake, reducing alcohol intake, reducing salt intake, reducing stress levels and reducing weight.

The most effective method of reducing raised blood pressure is a REDUCTION in WEIGHT.

High blood pressure can also be reduced by the use of medication. A specialist aviation medical doctor for a pilot must prescribe these.


(c) diet;

The essence of a healthy diet is to eat a wide variety of different foods and eat them in moderation.

Here are the recommendations from the NZ Heart Foundation:
  • Enjoy three meals a day, selecting from dishes that encourage you to eat plant foods and with little or no dairy fat, meat fat or deep fried foods
  • Choose fruits and/or vegetables at every meal and for most snacks
  • Select whole grains, wholegrain breads or high-fibre breakfast cereals, in place of white bread and low fibre varieties
  • Include fish or dried peas, beans and soy products, or a small serving of lean meat or skinned poultry, at one or more meals each day
  • Choose low-fat milk, low fat milk products, soy or legume products every day
  • Use nuts, seeds, avocado, oils or margarine instead of animal and coconut fats
  • Drink plenty of fluids each day, particularly water, and limit sugar-sweetened drinks and alcohol
  • Use only small amounts of sugar or salt when cooking and preparing meals, snacks or drinks (if any). Choose ready-prepared foods low in saturated fat, sugar and sodium
  • Mostly avoid or rarely include butter, deep-fried and fatty foods, and only occasionally choose sweet bakery products or pastries
(d) exercise;

Being active is key to being healthy . A great goal is aiming to be active in as many ways as possible throughout the day and reduce the amount of sitting for long periods.

Doing just 30 minutes of moderate exercise a day, even in 10 minute chunks, can help to reduce your risk of heart disease and diabetes as well as reducing your risk of developing a stroke and high blood pressure.

Along with healthy eating and not smoking, physical activity improves heart health by preventing heart disease as well as helping you to recover if you have had a heart event.

Lifelong physical activity, such as a brisk walk for as little as 30 minutes a day, is important for:

  • Preventing heart disease
  • Lowering your risk of high blood pressure, diabetes and stroke
  • Helping to fight the battle to quit smoking
  • Aiding cardiac rehabilitation
  • Establishing good heart healthy habits in children
  • Building stronger immunity
  • Reducing blood pressure in people who already have high blood pressure
  • Helping to reduce stress, tension, depression and anxiety
  • Helping to control weight
  • Improving overall health and well being, prolonging your optimal health

For each hour of regular exercise you get, you’ll gain about two hours of additional life expectancy, even if you don’t start until middle age!

(e) obesity;

The ideal weight for healthy adults, roughly speaking, is the weight they were at age 21 - the age when growing was complete, eating habits were probably not excessive and they were probably physically active. Weight in excess of this target is usually fat and primarily due to taking in more energy than is expended (over-eating). When this excess body fat reaches a certain level the individual is said to be obese.

Obesity can lead to a variety of diseases including hypertension, diabetes, and gout - which in turn can lead to coronary heart disease. The extra load on the joints can lead to arthritic problems too.


(f) smoking;

It is now undisputed in the medical world that a strong correlation exists between smoking and the risk of heart disease. A recent Australian study of 3000 people found that female smokers are 3.5 times more likely to suffer a heart attack than non-smokers, and males are 2.9 times more likely. Female smokers with a history of high blood pressure are 7.9 times more likely to suffer a heart attack, and males in this category 4.5 times.

A smoker who gives up will have the same risk category as someone who has never smoked with 2-3 years of giving up.

A few helpful hints for those trying to quit:
  • Do something different at times when you usually smoke
  • Avoid smokers and places where a lot of people smoke
  • Do more regular exercise
  • Try chewing gum or nibble on raw fruits or vegetables
  • Practise saying "No Thanks"
  • Get your friends to support you
  • Promise yourself a treat with the money you save
(g) respiratory tract infection/allergies (including colds, sinus, hay fever, influenza, asthma);

The Common Cold

A cold is a viral illness that usually lasts three to five days. There is no proven cure, or sure way of preventing getting a cold. Colds can potentially create problems for pilots.

A cold will make the pilot less able to think and react decisively, particularly in an emergency. A cold will cause congestion in the nose, sinuses and ears. In particular a cold can block the Eustachian tubes that connect the back of the throat to the inside of each eardrum and can also lead to blocked sinuses.

If these become blocked the pilot will be unable to equalise the pressures inside and outside during the climb or descent and severe pain can result because of the trapped air within them.

For the eardrum, although air can often expend and escape through the Eustachian tube during the climb, air cannot re-enter the tube during descent and severe pain, sometimes dizziness and a rupture of the eardrum can result. If the drum ruptures this can lead to problems with hearing and an unfitness to fly lasting several moths or longer.

Pilots are therefore advised not to fly when they have a heavy cold.

If the cold is light or the pilot is recovering from a cold a good pre-flight test of fitness to fly is to see whether the pilot can clear the ears using the VALSALVA MANOEUVRE. The pilot pinches the nose, tilts the head back slightly, closes the mouth and momentarily pressurises the throat by forcing air up from the lungs, causing the ears to pop.

If the ears cannot be cleared in this way or can only be cleared with much difficulty the pilot should not fly.

It is unwise to carry out the Valsalva manoeuvre during a severe cold or sinus infection, as this could force infection up into the middle ear or sinus and worsen the situation.

Acceptable Characteristics for non-reporting

Upper Respiratory Tract Infection:  
  • only ears, nose, sinus, throat, and/or conjunctiva are affected; and
  • there is no wheezing or shortness of breath; and
  • the condition lasts no more than two weeks; 

 Hay Fever

Hay fever is caused usually by an allergy to something like pollen, which is breathed in.

It causes congestion of the nose and sinuses, and symptoms similar to a cold.

It can be troublesome to a pilot in the same way as a cold can, and may lead to blocked sinuses and blocked and burst eardrums.

Unlike a cold, hay fever can be treated with an approved STEROID NASAL SPRAY.

Antihistamine pills like Coldrex are sedatives and can lead to sleepiness, impaired judgment, and reduced coordination. For this reason, pilots must not fly while under the influence of these medications



(h) food poisoning and gastroenteritis;

The most common cause of pilot incapacitation in flight is gastro-intestinal trouble, usually brought on by food or water poisoning.

You can’t tell contaminated food from its appearance, taste or smell.

Food poisoning usually shows up within about 12 hours of eating the tainted food.

Symptoms of gastrointestinal problems.

The first signs are usually a combination of the following:
  • SHAKINESS
  • HEADACHE
  • CHILL OR HOT FEELING
  • DIZZINESS
  • MUSCLE PAINS
  • STOMACH CRAMP AND NAUSEA.
  • THESE CAN BE FOLLOWED BY VOMITING OR DIARRHEA OR BOTH, AND CAN LEAD TO INABILITY TO CONTROL THE AIRCRAFT, OR EVEN PASSING OUT.

Generally, food poisoning causes some combination of nausea, vomiting, and diarrhoea that may or may not be bloody, sometimes with other symptoms. The symptoms last one to two days (24 - 48 hrs). The severity can vary, but this general description is consistent with the kind of incapacitation that is a major concern for pilots.

  • After eating tainted food, abdominal cramps, diarrhoea, and vomiting, can start as early as one hour in the case of staph and as late as 10 days in the case of campylobacter. It may take even longer to develop symptoms from parasite infections such as Giardia. Symptoms can last from one day up to a couple of months or longer, depending on the type of infection.
  • Vomiting, diarrhoea, sweating, dizziness, tearing in the eyes, excessive salivation, mental confusion, and stomach pain may be symptoms of chemical or toxin food poisoning such as that from poisonous mushrooms.
  • Partial loss of speech or blurred vision, muscle weakness, difficulty swallowing, dry mouth, muscle paralysis from the head down through the body, and vomiting may indicate botulism, a severe but very rare type of bacterial food poisoning
The primary causes of food poisoning.

Food poisoning is mainly caused by bacteria, viruses and, less commonly, by parasites. These organisms generally come from infected food handlers, animal faeces and soil.

Bacteria

Bacteria are microscopic organisms. There are many different types; some we want in our foods, others cause food to spoil, and some can make us sick. 

Bacteria that cause food poisoning are found mainly in animal faeces and in soil. Humans can have these bacteria in their faeces, mouth, nose and ears, and in infected sores.

Food poisoning bacteria do not change the smell, taste or appearance of the food. They include: Salmonella, Campylobacter jejuni, Staphylococcus aureus, certain strains of E. coli and Listeria monocytogenes.

Often food poisoning bacteria need to grow to large numbers in food to cause illness, which is why it is important to keep certain foods refrigerated or very hot. Others don’t need to grow to cause illness, which is why we need to protect food from contamination.

Viruses

Viruses are even smaller than bacteria. When in foods, even in tiny amounts, they can make us sick. Food borne viruses include hepatitis A and noroviruses.

Viruses are excreted in large numbers in the faeces – and for norovirus in particular, the vomit of infected people. If food handlers are infected they can contaminate food.

Food can also be contaminated if it is washed or grown in water containing human sewage. Oysters are particularly susceptible if grown in sewage-affected water.

Parasites

Parasites such as worms live inside people and animals. They are excreted in faeces and can contaminate meat during slaughter and fruit and vegetables grown in soil fertilised with manure. Examples include Cryptosporidium parvum and Giardia lamblia.

Preventing food poisoning

There are simple things you can do to prevent food poisoning; including washing and drying your hands thoroughly before preparing food and after handling raw meat; not preparing raw meat and vegetables/fruits on the same surface; not preparing foods for other people when you are sick, washing foods that aren’t going to be cooked (e.g. salad vegetables) and keeping certain foods very cold or very hot.

(i) neurological factors (including fits/epilepsy, brain injury, fainting, headaches, migraines);

A seizure, commonly referred to as a fit, is caused by a sudden, brief failure in the system that governs the brain cell communication system. Normally the nerve cells in the brain are constantly active, sending messages from one to the other about 80 times a second. The brain handles this much activity with ease. But in people with epilepsy, this activity can suddenly jump to 500 or more messages per second. This burst of hyperactivity can produce a seizure of one kind or another, the type depending on the part of the brain that becomes overactive.

The causes of about half the cases of epilepsy cannot be determined. In the other half, the condition can be attributed to various factors that can damage the brain's delicate electrochemical network such as severe blows to the head, a shortage of oxygen at birth, alcoholism, brain tumours and various infections of the brain. Anyone can develop epilepsy at any time of life.

Seizures are categorised as either minor or major. Minor epileptic fits often last only a few seconds, whereas major epilepsy involves convulsions and uncontrolled physical movements. In both cases consciousness is lost and consequently epilepsy is an absolute bar to holding a pilot's licence.

The presence of epilepsy may be detected through EEG (electro-encephalogram) testing of the brain waves (small voltages induced during normal brain activity)

Fainting (syncope) is a loss of consciousness caused by the reduction of the blood supply to the brain and can occur in healthy individuals. Typical causes are loss of blood, shock, lack of food and abnormal posture.

Examples of fainting when there is no trauma are standing up suddenly or standing to attention for long periods. Isolated incidents of fainting, providing the cause can be identified, will not normally affect fitness to fly. Repeated fainting, or fainting due to physical or medical problems will require treatment.

Headaches and Migraines

Headaches are among the most common medical ailments. Headaches can impair a pilot's abilities, and in rare occurrences, headaches have been cited as a factor in aviation accidents.

Many treatments can be effective in relieving headache symptoms; nevertheless, in some circumstance, either the headache or the treatment can result in loss of medical certificate.



Migraines are one type of vascular headache (a headache involving blood vessels in the brain). They are typically recurrent, intense, throbbing headaches that often only affect one side of the head. Migraines may be accompanied by loss of appetite, nausea, vomiting, increased sensitivity to light, inability to concentrate, and visual disturbances such as blind spots or the appearance of flashing lights.

Migraine headaches can be triggered by a variety of factors, including lack of food, lack of sleep, exposure to elevated levels of light or sound, anxiety, stress and fatigue.

Civil Aviation Authority of New Zealand General Direction Temporary Medical Conditions.

Acceptable Characteristics for non-reporting
  • the headaches are occurring no more frequently than four times per year; and
  • the headaches are not distracting or incapacitating; and
  • there are no visual disturbance, nausea, or vomiting; and
  • there is no weakness or alteration in sensations (i.e. numbness, tingling, pinsand-needle, pain) anywhere in the body; and
  • the headaches have not been diagnosed or suspected to be migraines; and
  • the headaches do not require medication other than occasional acceptable medication; and
  • the headaches resolve quickly (e.g. within 20 minutes when taking acceptable medication; and
  • no flying or ATC duty is initiated while having a headache

(j) emotional factors (including depression and anxiety);

Certain emotionally upsetting events, including a serious argument, death of a family member, separation or divorce, loss of job, and financial catastrophe, can render a pilot unable to fly an aircraft safely. The emotions of anger, depression, and anxiety from such events not only decrease alertness but also may lead to taking risks that border on self-destruction. Any pilot who experiences an emotionally upsetting event should not fly until satisfactorily recovered from it.

Neurosis (anxiety, depression, phobia, obsession) can be treated and controlled, but flying is prohibited during such treatment. A return to flying status is possible after a satisfactory period of treatment and recovery.


Psychiatric diseases;

Psychiatric illness may be subdivided into two basic forms - psychosis and neurosis.

Psychosis (loss of contact with reality) results in permanent denial of any form of flying licence.

A psychosis, bipolar disorder and some types of personality disorder automatically disqualify a pilot from obtaining a medical certificate.

However the CAA encourages pilots to seek help if they have a mental health condition since most, if treated, do not disqualify a pilot from flying.

(k) dehydration;

We all know that proper hydration is vital to good health. In the case of flying, it is even more critical. Not only is dehydration more likely at altitude and its effects more pronounced, but the consequences of losing one's competitive edge are that much more life-threatening when at the controls of a flying machine. What is dehydration?

Start with the fact that 70 percent of the body weight is water based. And 87 percent of that is inside the cell (intracellular). The "functional water" is required for oxygen enrichment and for maintaining the pH balance.

Water is vital for blood, digestive juices, sweat and tears. Any discrepancy will lead to complications with delivery of these "humours," including oxygen to the body cells, creating a relative hypoxia.

Complaints include: Nausea, thirst, sore eyes, coughing, dry nose and throat, minor bleeding from the nose, exhaustion, muscle and joint aches, anginal pain, migraine, restlessness and most importantly symptoms such as confusion, paranoia and anxiety.

Dehydration can occur as a result of high altitude, excessive exercise, sweating and water deprivation. The balance to maintain optimal body water level is coordinated by the kidneys, mostly by concentrating urine.
Aircraft flying at high altitudes with pressurised cabins take ambient air from outside the aircraft and pressurise it to keep the cabin at "low altitudes". the humidity of air reduces markedly with altitude and so cabin air is usually extremely dry.

A study of Air New Zealand 767's found that the air on the flight deck has a relative humidity of approximately 5% and that almost all of that is contributed by the pilots through respiration and perspiration. (levels of humidity should be around 40-60% for optimum comfort). It can be seen then, that in this environment the body will quickly become dehydrated.


Fluid loss of a sedentary person such as a pilot would be between 200-300ml per hour. The loss from flight attendants would possibly be 300 mls depending on their activity level. Military pilots in full flight suites would conceivably be as high as 600ml per hr.

The cure for dehydration is the regular intake of water, so drink plenty of water before, during and after flight. And count at least five 8-ounce glasses of water as part of your daily goal for optimal health.
