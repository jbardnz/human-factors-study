10.14 Entrapped Gases


It is important we have a basic understanding of the human body and the things that can affect our performance.

At altitude there are many differences from what we are used to on the surface of the earth. The most important difference is the pressure change.


10.14.2 Define Barotrauma

Every time a pilot takes off from the earth’s surface and climbs up into the atmosphere there is a decrease in the surrounding air pressure. Descending back down to the earth’s surface the reverse will occur.

Whenever there is a drop in air pressure, any gas that is trapped will tend to EXPAND.

Several cavities in the body naturally contain gas that can become trapped in a climb, and pain may be experienced. This pain is known as BAROTRAUMA.

There are three areas in the human body which contain trapped gas. These are behind the eardrum in the Middle Ear, the Sinuses, and Stomach.


10.14.4 Explain the causes of barotrauma. 

With an increase in altitude and the corresponding decrease in pressure, any trapped gas tends to expand. There will be an INCREASE in pressure differential across the wall of whatever is trapping it. As the air pressure increases again when we come back toward earth, any gas that is trapped will tend to CONTRACT, and creates a VACUUM.


10.14.6 Describe the symptoms of barotrauma. 

The most common symptoms of ear barotrauma may include:
  • Feeling of pressure in the ear.
  • Ear pain.
  • Dizziness.
  • Feeling like you have a blocked ear.
  • Bleeding from the ears or into the middle ear.
  • Ringing in your ears.
  • Hearing loss.
Pain behind the eyes or in the stomach and/or in a tooth are symptoms of barotrauma in those areas.


10.14.8 Describe the effects of barotrauma on various parts of the body. 

There are three main areas in the human body which contain trapped gas. These are behind the eardrum in the Middle Ear, the Sinuses and Stomach. You can also get barometric pressure related dental pain (toothache).


We can experience pain in any of these three areas on the climb OR the descent, but are more likely to experience pain in the middle ear (Otic Barotrauma) and sinuses on the DESCENT, and the sinuses and stomach on the ASCENT.

Ear injury barotrauma happens when something blocks your eustachian tube or it becomes inflamed. Your eustachian tube runs from the back of your middle ear to the back of your throat. It helps maintain equal air pressure on both sides of your ear drum by letting outside air into your middle ear.

When air can’t reach your middle ear, air pressure builds up in the space behind your ear drum, causing the pressure in your middle ear to become unbalanced. The air pressure difference can make your ears hurt.

Middle ear barotrauma

The sinuses, like other air-filled cavities, are susceptible to barotrauma if their openings become obstructed. Swelling of the mucous membrane lining, caused by an infection or an allergic reaction (hay fever) may obstruct the sinus openings. This can result in pain as well as epistaxis (nosebleed). The frontal sinus are most affected.

Frontal sinus

Air trapped in the stomach expands during ascent, causing abdominal fullness, cramps, pain, belching, and flatulence; these symptoms are self-limited. Gastrointestinal (GI) rupture rarely occurs.

Stomach barotrauma

If air is trapped under a tooth filling then this can expand causing tooth pain.



How barotrauma can be prevented. 

Do not fly if you are suffering from cold or flu symptoms such as a blocked nose or ears.

If you’re flying on an airplane, taking an over-the-counter decongestant before you take off can help protect your ears (be careful that medications are approved for pilot use) and sinus areas.

If your ears or sinus are still affected by air pressure changes, try chewing a piece of gum, yawning or blowing your nose.

To help avoid gases building up in the bowel don't eat spicy foods, bake beans and avoid beer - all of which ferment more than most food and drink.


How barotrauma can be treated. 

The air in the middle ear is maintained at ambient pressure via the EUSTACHIAN TUBE that connects the interior of the middle ear to the nasal passage. There should be easy passage of air when pressure needs to equalise (in a climb or decent). A person with a cold has a blocked Eustachian and this can have serious consequences.



To open the Eustachian Tube, we must start moving the jaw (sucking hard boiled sweets can achieve this) or by using the VALSALVA MANOEUVRE.

To Valsalva, hold the nose and close the mouth and pressurise the throat briefly. This should result in a popping sensation which should clear the ears.



The use of nasal sprays may help in clearing passage blockages.

Massaging the area of a stomach pain may help.
