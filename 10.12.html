                                                 10.12 Hyperventilation


10.12.2 Define Hyperventilation

Hyperventilation is rapid or deep breathing, usually caused by anxiety or panic. This over breathing, as it is sometimes called, may actually leave you feeling breathless.

When you breathe, you inhale oxygen and exhale carbon dioxide. Excessive breathing may lead to low levels of carbon dioxide in your blood, which causes a chemical imbalance in the blood and many of the symptoms that you may feel if you hyperventilate. 

The brain reacts to this low level of carbon dioxide by limiting the blood flow.




10.12.4 Explain the causes of hyperventilation. 

Causes of Hyperventilation are:
  • EXCESSIVE WORRY
  • ANXIETY
  • FEAR
  • PAIN
  • LOUD NOISE
  • VIBRATION
  • EXCESSIVE HEAT
  • AIRSICKNESS
  • STRAINING AGAINST G-FORCES

10.12.6 Describe the symptoms of hyperventilation. 

The symptoms of Hyperventilation include:
  • RAPID BREATHING
  • UNPLEASANT LIGHT- HEADEDNESS, DIZZINESS
  • NUMBNESS & TINGLING OF THE HANDS & FEET, & AROUND THE MOUTH
  • HAND MUSCLES CAN GO INTO SPASMS
  • PAIN
  • FAINTING
  • RED FACE (SOMETIMES)

10.12.8 Describe how hyperventilation can be treated. 

The symptoms of hyperventilation subside within a few minutes after the rate and depth of breathing are consciously brought back under control. Normal breathing is 12-16 breaths per minute.

Place a paper bag over the mouth and try to get the person to regulate their breathing. This can help to build up carbon dioxide levels in the blood again.


If the source of anxiety is an emergency that must be dealt with immediately, take a moment and remember your emergency procedure training.

Because most of the early symptoms of hyperventilation are identical to those of hypoxia, it must always be treated as if it is hypoxia – since this is life threatening.

Supplying a hyperventilating victim with oxygen will not harm them, but giving a hypoxic victim a paper bag to breathe into will kill them.


10.12.10 Describe the differences between hyperventilation and hypoxia. 

Hyperventilation can be a symptom of hypoxia or a condition on its own. 

Hyperventilation and hypoxia can occur together since an early compensatory reaction to hypoxia is an increased respiratory rate.

To distinguish between hypoxia and hyperventilation is very difficult. An excellent way to train airmen in the recognition of each is to experience the signs and symptoms in an altitude chamber.

A person suffering from hyperventilation tends to take deeper breaths than usual and breathes more frequently.

Unlike a collapse from hypoxia however, the patient will collapse into unconsciousness, and the rate and depth of breathing should return to normal and the patient will recover.



Hyperventilation results in a significant decrease in carbon dioxide content (CO2) in the blood. Hypoxia is a result of a decrease in oxygen (O2) levels in the blood.
