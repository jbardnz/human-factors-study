10.16 Decompression Sickness



10.16.2 Define decompression sickness.

Decompression sickness, also called generalised barotrauma or the bends, refers to injuries caused by a rapid decrease in the pressure that surrounds you, of either air or water. It can occur during high-altitude or unpressurised air travel.

Decompression sickness occurs when rapid pressure reduction  (eg, during ascent to altitude) causes nitrogen gas previously dissolved in blood or tissues to form bubbles in blood vessels.


10.16.4 Explain the causes of decompression sickness. 

Because atmospheric pressure reduces at altitude, the body tissues can no longer retain atmospheric nitrogen in solution. As a result, nitrogen bubbles enter the blood circulation and form obstructions in blood vessels. 

The nitrogen comes out of solution in the blood in much the same way that gas bubbles appear out of soft drink when opened, as the pressure of the liquid has been reduced. Dissolved gases emerge from solution as bubbles.


Nitrogen bubbles in the blood vessel


During flight below 10,000 ft AMSL nitrogen causes no problems for the pilot.

In prolonged unpressurised flight above 18,000ft AMSL nitrogen bubbles can start to cause problems. The higher the pilot goes in an unpressurised aircraft the more likely it is that problems caused by nitrogen will be encountered. Above 25,000ft AMSL the likelihood becomes quite strong.

Decompression sickness or the bends is caused by Nitrogen forming as bubbles in the blood and tissues.


10.16.6 Describe the symptoms of decompression sickness. 

Decompression sickness can take various forms depending on where the bubbles of nitrogen form.

The most characteristic symptoms of the bends include pain in the large joints resulting from the pressure nitrogen bubbles place on tendons and nerves, together with spasms of the blood vessels.  Formation in the joints (the bends) causes pain aggravated by movement. Bubbles can also form in the skin, the nervous system or the lungs.

Skin symptoms (itchiness) are not serious but they can be an indication that bubbles are forming elsewhere. Joint pain is the most common symptom. Shortness of breath or a feeling of suffocation is a result of nitrogen bubbles forming in the lungs. Neurologic symptoms are very rare but include the inability to speak or hear, loss of orientation or partial paralysis.

Symptoms Of The Bends Include:
  • JOINT PAIN(S)
  • ITCHINESS
  • NUMBNESS
  • TINGLING AND PARALYSIS OF PARTS O F THE BODY
  • PROBLEMS WITH CO-ORDINATION AND MOVEMENT
  • UNCONSCIOUSNESS
Decompression Sickness (DCS) symptoms



10.16.8 Explain how decompression sickness can be prevented. 

Pressurised cabins are good for the prevention of decompression sickness.

Avoid flying within 24 hours of deep sea diving.

Pre-flight inhalation of pure oxygen eliminates nitrogen from the system, and is a proven preventative measure for the bends.

Exercise reduces the threshold for manifestation of decompression sickness.


10.16.10 Describe how decompression sickness can be treated. 

The only treatment for an attack of the bends is to return the victim to a higher-pressure environment so the nitrogen bubbles are forced back into the blood solution.

This means urgent RECOMPRESSION in a COMPRESSION CHAMBER, or dive chamber.




The higher pressure that has forced the nitrogen bubbles to re-dissolve is then slowly reduced back to sea level pressure – however at a rate which retains the N2 bubbles in solution.

If the bends is discovered in flight then an immediate descent and landing is needed.

Transporting A Bends Victim

If the diver needs to be flown for treatment, this can be done:
  • NOT ABOVE 500 FT AMSL
  • IN A PRESSURISED AIRCRAFT – AT A CABIN ALTITUDE BELOW 500 FT IDEALLY SEA LEVEL OR BELOW.

10.16.12 Explain the dangers of flying after diving. 

Flying and diving both involve pressure changes to the body. For divers, the use of air under pressure for breathing increases the quantity of nitrogen in solution in the body. When a diver using SCUBA equipment ascends, and when a pilot climbs there is a decompression about the body. If the ascent from a dive is done too quickly the sudden change in pressure causes the nitrogen to come out of solution and form bubbles in the body, causing decompression sickness (the BENDS)

ELDERLY AND OVERWEIGHT PILOTS ARE MORE SUSCEPTIBLE TO THE BENDS.

If a diver’s ascends to the water’s surface and shortly thereafter goes flying in an aircraft the diver will have an increased chance of getting the bends. Even though the increased nitrogen solution may not have been sufficient to cause decompression sickness after a dive, the further decompression associated with a flight at altitude may be sufficient to bring on an attack.

AN AIRLINER CREW SPENT A DAY SCUBA DIVING AT DEPTHS BETWEEN 20 AND 30 FT.

THEY THEN CREWED A FLIGHT BACK TO THEIR HOME BASE.

THE AIRCRAFT WAS PRESSURISED, WITH A CABIN ALTITUDE OF 8000 FT, BUT BOTH PILOTS DEVELOPED SEVERE DECOMPRESSION SICKNESS , MANAGING TO LAND THE AIRCRAFT ONLY WITH GREAT DIFFICULTY.

IF THE AIRCRAFT HAD LOST PRESSURISATION, THE EFFECTS ON THIS CREW WOULD HAVE BEEN CATASTROPHIC.


The approximate required times between diving at various depths and flying. 

Rules For Flying After Diving
  • IF POSSIBLE, DON’T FLY FOR AT LEAST 24 HOURS AFTER THE LAST DIVE.
  • IF THE DIVE IS LESS THAN 10 METRES , DON’T FLY FOR 12 HOURS .
  • IF THE DIVE IS GREATER THAN 10 METRES , DON’T FLY FOR 24 HOURS .
  • IF THE DIVE IS GREATER THAN 35 METRES , DON’T FLY FOR 48 HOURS .
  • IF YOU INTEND FLYING ABOVE 8,000FT AMSL, DON’T FLY FOR 48 HOURS.
Note: Most cabins are pressurised to 8,000 ft PA.
