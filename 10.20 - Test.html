Question 4:

Which procedure is recommended to prevent or overcome spatial disorientation?

Reduce head and eye movement to the greatest possible extent
Rely on your kinaesthetic sense from Proprioceptors (Nerve Receptors)
Rely entirely on the indication of the flight instruments
Rely in muscle and gut sensations to enhance spacial orientation
Correct!
Incorrect
Answer: Rely entirely on the indication of the flight instruments - 10.22.10 The best way to overcome the effects of spatial disorientation is to rely entirely on the airplanes flight instruments and/or ground references (if available) and not on body sensations. Option one is incorrect because head and eye movement has little effect on spatial disorientation. Option two is incorrect because relying on the kinaesthetic sense encourages, rather than prevents, spatial disorientation.

Question 6:

Define an illusion that is associated with an aircraft in a constant turn whereby the sense of turning stops after about ten seconds after a turn is established. Then there is a strong sense of turning in the opposite direction when the turn is stopped.

Somatogravic illusion
Somatogyral illusion
Ground lighting illusion
Autokinesis
Correct!
Incorrect
Answer: Somatogyral illusion - 10.22.14 .A somatogyral illusion is associated with an aircraft in a constant turn (gyro = turn). The sense of turning stops after about ten seconds after a turn is established. There is a strong sense of turning in the opposite direction when the turn is stopped. (Both sensations are illusions.) This is readily over-ridden by visual horizon cues. Option one is incorrect because Somatogravic illusion is due to a rapid acceleration during take off that can create the illusion of being in a nose up attitude. The disorientated pilot could push the aeroplane into nose low or dive attitude. Option three is incorrect because a ground lighting illusion refers to lights on a straight path, such as a road, being mistaken by a pilot for runway or approach lights. Option four is incorrect because in the dark, a static light will appear to move about when stared at for many seconds (autokinesis). The disorientated pilot may lose control of the aircraft in attempting to align it with the light. Option one is incorrect because somatogravic illusion occurs with a rapid acceleration during tafeoff, creating the illusion of being in a nose up attitude.. This is the main cause of the graveyard spiral, which killed JFK jr and other pilots.

Question 7:

The illusion of being in a nose up attitude, which may occur during a rapid acceleration take-off, is known as?

Inversion illusion
Autokinesis
Somatogravic illusion
Pressure vertigo
Correct!
Incorrect
Answer: Somatogravic illusion - 10.22.14 A rapid acceleration during take off can create the illusion of being in a nose up attitude. The disorientated pilot could push the aeroplane into nose low or dive attitude. Option one is incorrect because the inversion illusion results from an abrupt change from climb to straight and level flight, which can create an illusion of tumbling backwards. Option two is incorrect because autokinesis refers to a static light appearing to move about when stared at for many seconds in the dark (something I see often) option four is not correct as it describes what happens if one ear equalises, but the other doesn’t, then a form of pressure vertigo can be experienced as dizziness.

Question 16:

Balance organs in the inner ear such as the semi circular canals:

Can send confusing signals to the brain and should be ignored
Are able to sense linear acceleration in the three different planes (pitch, roll and yaw)
Correct!
Incorrect
Answer: Are the main organs used for spatial orientation - 10.22.6 Option three is incorrect because while they can send confusing signals to the brain they most certainly should not be ignored. Option four is incorrect because, it is the Otolith organs which are a jelly-like mass sitting on top of a group of vertical hairs that senses both head/body tilt and linear acceleration. The semicircular canals sense ANGULAR ACCELERATION in three different planes, similar to those of an aircraft.

Question 17:

While making prolonged constant rate turns under IFR condition, an abrupt head movement can create the illusion or rotation on an entirely different axis. This is known as?

Inversion illusion
The leans
Autokinesis
Coriolis illusion
Correct!
Incorrect
Answer: Coriolis illusion - 10.22.14 Abrupt head movement in a prolonged constant rate turn that has ceased stimulating the motion sensing system (in your ears) can create the illusion or rotation or movement on an entirely different axis. The disorientated pilot could maneuver the aircraft in a dangerous attitude in an attempt to stop rotation. Not making sudden, extreme head movements may prevent this effect, particularly while making prolonged constant rate turns under IFR conditions. Option one is incorrect because the inversion illusion results from an abrupt change from climb to straight and level flight, which can create an illusion of tumbling backwards. Option two is incorrect because the term “the leans” refers to an abrupt correction of a banked attitude which has been entered too slowly to stimulate the motion sensing system in the inner ear and can create the illusion of bank in the opposite direction. Option three is incorrect because autokinesis refers to a static light appearing to move about when stared at for many seconds in the dark (something I see often)
