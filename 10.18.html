10.18 Vision and Visual Perception



One of the most important medical requirements for a pilot is to be able to see, and see well. Aviation personnel must be aware of and know how to compensate effectively for the following: physical deficiency or self-imposed stress, such as smoking, which limits night-vision capability; visual-cue deficiencies; visual limitations, consisting of degraded visual acuity, dark adaptation, and colour and depth perception. For example, at night, the unaided eye has degraded visual acuity. Aircrew members must learn and effectively apply proper night-vision viewing techniques to compensate for this limitation.

Looking, Seeing And Recognising

Vision is a complex process involving LOOKING, SEEING and eventually RECOGNISING. This process can take up to 2 seconds.

It uses the eyes and also the vision centre and memory parts of the brain.

Human vision is relied upon very heavily both on the ground and in the air for normal body orientation in relation to the earth’s horizon. Loss of sight of the horizon can bring about severe problems of ‘disorientation’.


10.18.2 Identify the following eye structure components:

The Eye

The eye detects light energy of different colours.

When light energy enters the eye, it passes through THE CORNEA (the tough protective lining of the eye), and through THE LENS, which together focus the incoming rays against THE RETINA (the back of the eye).


If the light energy is strong enough, it will cause a light chemical reaction within the retina, generating a nerve signal that passes from the retina to the vision centre located at the back of the brain.

The eyes are able to function at very bright levels of light and can also detect very low levels of light. This is possible by THE PUPIL, the variable hole at the front of the eye. It is also made possible because the retina has two different types of light receptors.


You will be required to identify the following eye structure components:

(a) lens

The lens is a clear, curved disk that sits behind the iris and in front of the vitreous of the eye. It is the part of the eye that focuses light and images from the outer world, bending them onto the retina.


(b) cornea

The cornea is your eye’s clear, protective outer layer. Along with the sclera (the white of your eye), it serves as a barrier against dirt, germs, and other things that can cause damage.

(c) retina

The retina contains millions of light-sensitive cells (rods and cones) and other nerve cells that receive and organise visual information. Your retina sends this information to your brain through your optic nerve, enabling you to see.


(d) fovea

The fovea or fovea centralis is a small depression at the centre of the retina that's responsible for central vision. It's the point at which visual acuity is at its highest.

Visual acuity is the ability to identify the details of objects when you look at them.

The fovea contains many cones (the cells that receive visual information). This is why the fovea is responsible for clear vision. It is a small, central pit composed of closely packed cones.

(e) optic nerve disc

Is the circular area in the back of the inside of the eye where the optic nerve connects to the retina. Also called the optic nerve head. 

The optic nerve carries sensory nerve impulses from the more than one million ganglion cells of the retina toward the visual centres in the brain.

The optic nerve begins at the optic disk, a structure that is 1.5 mm (0.06 inch) in diameter and is located at the back of the eye. The optic disk forms from the convergence of ganglion cell output fibres (called axons) as they pass out of the eye.

This is also called the blind spot. [see below for further regarding the blind spot]



(f) cone cells

Cones are a type of photoreceptor cell in the retina. They give us our colour vision. Cones are concentrated in the centre of our retina in an area called the macula and help us see fine details. The retina has approximately 120 million rods and 6 million cones.

There are three types of cone cells:

  • Red-sensing cones (60 percent)
  • Green-sensing cones (30 percent) and
  • Blue-sensing cones (10 percent)
The Cones are found concentrated in the centre of the retina. THE FOVEA is made up entirely of cones. The cones are responsible for:
  • RECEIVING BRIGHT LIGHT ENERGY
  • SHARP VISION OF FINE DETAIL
  • REGISTERING COLOUR
(g) rod cells

There are approximately 120 million rod cells in the eye.

There are no rods in the fovea, but they are located throughout the rest of the retina. The concentration progressively increases toward the peripheral area. Rods are responsible for:

  • RECEIVING WEAK LIGHT ENERGY
  • NIGHT VISION
  • DETECTING MOVEMENT
  • PERIPHERAL VISION

The cones and rods combined provide a good general field of vision.

Rods detect movement in the outer field of vision, towards which the pilot will instinctively look (turn the head and eyes), thereby bringing to bear the much sharper and fine detailed vision provided by the cones located centrally in the retina.

Two eyes allow three-dimensional vision out to a distance of about 10 METRES. Beyond this vision becomes two-dimensional (binocular depth perception is lost). Distances can still be estimated fairly well because of the already known comparative size, shape alignment and perspective of objects lying within the field of vision.

Humans are bi-focal. This means that when we are looking straight ahead, we are able to see a 20° sector of the total field of vision.

10.18.6 Describe the limitations of the eye in terms of: 

(a) the ability to discern objects at night; 

Night Vision

At night, the unaided eye has degraded visual acuity. Also at night, the horizon may be hard to discern due to dark terrain and misleading light patterns on the ground.


When light levels decrease, the focusing mechanism of the eye may move toward a resting position and make the eye more myopic - called “dark focus.”. These factors become important when pilots rely on terrain features during unaided night flights. Practicing good light discipline is very important and helps pilots to retain their night adaptation. Keeping the cockpit lighting on dim allows the pilot to better identify outside details, unmarked hazards such as towers less than 200' AGL, and unimproved landing sites with no hazard lighting. 

A simple exercise that shows the difference in light contrast would be to go out to a very dark road and turn the dash board lights down very low or off and let your eyes adjust to the ambient light level. Then, turn the dash board lights up and note how the outside features disappear. The same concept applies to cockpit lighting and being able to see the surrounding terrain and obstacles.

Diet and general physical health have an impact on how well a person can see in the dark. Deficiencies in vitamins A and C have been shown to reduce night acuity. Other factors, such as carbon monoxide poisoning, smoking, alcohol, and certain drugs can greatly decrease night vision. Lack of oxygen can also decrease night vision as the eye requires more oxygen per weight than any other part of the body.

Good night visual acuity is needed for collision avoidance. Night scanning, like day scanning, uses a series of short, regularly spaced eye movements in 10° sectors. Unlike day scanning, however, off-center viewing is used to focus objects on the rods rather than the fovea blindspot. 

When looking at an object, avoid staring at it too long. If staring at an object without moving the eyes, the retina becomes accustomed to the light intensity and the image begins to fade. To keep it clearly visible, new areas in the retina must be exposed to the image. Small, circular eye movements help eliminate the fading. Also, move the eyes more slowly from sector to sector than during the day to prevent blurring.

(b) the ability to discern objects in daylight, including wires and other aircraft; 

Outside of a 10° cone, visual acuity drops 90%. 


Most cockpit windscreen configurations severely limit the view available to the pilot. The available view is frequently interrupted by obstructions such as window-posts which totally obscure some parts of the view and make other areas visible to only one eye. Window-posts, windscreen crazing and dirt can act as ‘focaltraps’ and cause the pilot to involuntarily focus at a very short distance even when attempting to scan for traffic. 

Direct glare from the sun and veiling glare reflected from windscreens can effectively mask some areas of the view.

The contrast between an aircraft and its background can be significantly reduced by atmospheric effects/ even in conditions of good visibility. In addition, complex backgrounds such as ground features or clouds hamper the identification of aircraft via a visual effect known as ‘contour interaction’. This occurs when background contours interact with the form of the aircraft/ producing a less distinct image. 

In the absence of visual cues/ the eyes focus at a resting distance of around half a metre (empty field myopia)

An object which is smaller than the eye’s acuity threshold is unlikely to be detected and even less likely to be identified as an approaching aircraft. The pilot’s functional visual field contracts under conditions of stress or increased workload. The resulting ‘tunnel vision’ reduces the chance that an approaching aircraft will be seen in peripheral vision. 

(c) poor lighting; 

Flying in the dark or at dusk may precipitate visual difficulties. The widened aperture of the dilated pupil will reduce the depth of field of the lens, and the absence of colour will tend to accentuate any refractive errors.  

Emmetropes or myopes will tend to shift towards myopia at night. Presbyopes tend to have difficulty due to the monochromatic nature of vision in such conditions (see below for explanations of these conditions)

Pilots with cataracts will find that the reduced light transmission by the lens will cause difficulty seeing in low-intensity light environments, and that scattering of light will make them unduly sensitive to glare.

(d) glare; 

Glare occurs when the scene viewed has high contrast and the brightest light source is excessively bright (particularly if its position causes its light to fall directly onto the fovea). 

Glare at high altitudes will tend to be reflected from below, whereas at low levels glare from the sun will come from above, reflecting off shiny surfaces. When flying at altitude the pilot is exposed to light of very high intensity. Bright sunlight reflected from cloud tops can be particularly annoying due to the lack of natural protection offered by the forehead, eyebrows and eyelashes.

The contrast between the glare of a very bright outside environment and the darker cockpit interior make it difficult to read instruments and charts inside the cockpit.

The Effects of Glare may be categorised as: 

(i) Disability glare (where light internally reflected within the eye either impairs or completely disables normal vision). 

(ii) Discomfort glare (where the light causes eye discomfort and accelerated fatigue). 

(iii) Veiling glare (where the light obscures normal details in the visual field). 

In conditions of glare, it is recommended to protect your eyes with high quality sunglasses that :
  • SHOULD BE IMPACT RESISTANT
  • TRANSMIT 10 TO 15 % OF LIGHT
  • FILTER OUT ULTRAVIOLET RAY S
  • SHOULD NOT BE WORN IN DECREASED LIGHT
Polarised sunglasses may produce some areas with total vision loss so are not recommended for flying. 

(e) lack of contrast; 

It is common in the aviation environment to have very large and swift changes in light and contrast levels, to which the eye must adapt. 

Fog, haze and dust can affect perceptual judgments of speed and distance. The effects are the result of reduced contrast. We see objects, not based on their brightness or darkness, but on their difference between the object brightness and background. Fog lowers contrast substantially, causing objects to become fainter and harder to see. The main cause of this is that light hits the moisture droplets or dust particles and scatters. 

(f) the blind spot; 

Small nerves from behind the retina carry the light-generated nerve signals to the visual centre of the brain from the rods and cones. The thousands of small nerves gather together to form one much larger nerve known as the OPTIC NERVE.

Where the optic nerve leaves the retina (the optic nerve disk) there is no room for cones or rods to exist here, therefore any light energy falling in this area does not reach the visual centre of the brain.

This means that each eye has a BLIND SPOT.


We don’t continually see this blind spot, as the brain fills in what it thinks should be there automatically. When scanning, the pilot must always consider artificial blind spots such as spars and wings.

Look at the cross and the dot may appear in your blind spot - move towards or away from the image to find your blind spot:
Night Blind Spot

The night blind spot should not be confused with the day blind spot. The night blind spot occurs when the fovea becomes inactive under low-level light conditions. The night blind spot involves an area from 5 to 10 degrees wide in the centre of the visual field. If an object is viewed directly at night, it may not be seen because of the night blind spot; if the object is detected, it will fade away when stared at for longer than two seconds. The size of the night blind spot increases as the distance between the eyes and the object increases. Therefore, the night blind spot can hide larger objects as the distance between the observer and the object increases

(g) colour perception; 

Colours are detected in the central foveal region of the retina by the cone receptors. The average eye can distinguish over one hundred single wavelength colours and one thousand shades.

Aircrew require colour discrimination for: 
  • Identification of colour light signals and navigation lights. 
  • Airport beacons. 
  • Approach lights.
  • Runway lights (end lights, centre lights, stopping distance markers). 
  • Taxi strips. 
  • VASI (Visual Approach Slope Indicator) and PAPI (Precision ASI).
  • Instrument panel (navigation and warning lights). 
  • Instrument panel colour TV-type instrument displays, and radar. 
  • Instrument zone markers on panels.
  • Flares. 
  • Map and chart reading. 
  • Colour differences in terrain.
There are some eyes that cannot distinguish any colours at all, but total colour blindness is very rare. DEFECTIVE COLOUR VISION is difficulty in distinguishing between red and green. However new advances in aviation means loss of colour vision is not prohibitive to your flying career.


10.18.8 Explain the process of dark adaptation. 

At night, the unaided eye has degraded visual acuity. Aircrew members must learn and effectively apply proper night-vision viewing techniques to compensate for this limitation.

The eyes can adapt to low levels of light, but this takes time to occur.


10.18.10 State the normal time for full night vision adaptation. 

If the cones are not stimulated by sufficient light, they will cease to send information to the brain. This shutting down process takes about SEVEN MINUTES of low-level lighting. While the cones are shutting down, the rods are taking over.

The rods take about 30 MINUTES to reach their most sensitive.

It TAKES 10 minutes for HALF of YOUR NIGHT VISION,
and 30 minutes for TOTAL DARK ADAPTATION.

Pilots taking off or landing at night by use of visual outside reference should ensure that their vision is adequately night adapted before doing so.


10.18.12 Identify precautionary actions to protect night vision adaptation. 

Instrument, cockpit, and rear cargo area overhead lights (if applicable) should be adjusted to the lowest readable level that allows instruments, charts, and maps to be interpreted without prolonged staring or exposure. If the eyes become blurry, blinking more frequently often helps.

One second or less of exposure to bright white light will destroy night adaptation for a further 30 MINUTES.


10.18.14  Describe the factors associated with the selection of suitable sunglasses for flying. 

It is recommended not to use polarised sunglasses for flying as while they are useful for blocking reflected light from horizontal surfaces such as water, it can reduce or eliminate the visibility of instruments that incorporate anti-glare filters. They may also interfere with visibility through the windscreen. Lenses of polycarbonate material are preferred because of their impact-resistance and ability to absorb ultra-violet and infra-red rays and they should not distort colour perception.


10.18.16 Describe the visual system resting state focus and its effects on object detection. 

Cruise flight in transport aircraft involves relatively few manoeuvres and long periods of flight on constant heading and altitude. In visual cruise flight a common illusion affects the evaluation of relative altitude. A distant range of mountains which appears higher than the aircraft is revealed as being lower at closer range. The same is true of distant cloud tops and approaching aircraft. This can lead the pilots of both aircraft into initiating unnecessary avoiding action which might, of itself, cause a collision.

For a target to be detected by the rods in the eye's periphery, it must be moving, flashing, glinting, or very large. Unfortunately another aeroplane on a collision course may possess none of these critical characteristics until the moment before impact when it suddenly fills the whole windscreen.

In the absence of visual cues/ the eyes focus at a resting distance of around half a metre (empty field myopia). Because the natural focal point is so close it takes an effort to focus at greater distances, particularly in the absence of visual cues. We stare but see nothing.


10.18.18 Explain effective visual search techniques. 

Scanning is the process of looking, seeing and recognising. It takes about one and a half seconds for this to occur. This fact is important when scanning the sky outside looking for other aircraft because if insufficient time is allowed the pilot will not see anything!

Effective scanning is accomplished by a series of short, regularly-spaced eye movements that bring successive areas of the sky into the central visual field. Each movement should not exceed 10°, and each area should be observed for at least two seconds to enable detection.

Side-to-side scanning method

Start at the far left of your visual area and make a methodical sweep to the right, pausing very briefly in each block of the viewing area to focus your eyes. At the end of the scan, return to and scan the instrument panel and then repeat the external scan.



Because of factors that can adversely affect vision such as glare and contrast, a scan time of TWO SECONDS PER 20-DEGREE SECTOR is used.

An effective division between Looking outside for 15 seconds, then inside for 5 seconds, then repeat is also recommended. Studies show that the amount of time the pilot spends on visual tasks inside the cabin should be no more than one-fourth to one-third of scan time outside, or no more than 4 to 5 seconds on the instrument panel for every 15 seconds of outside scanning when in VFR conditions. 

This recognition process makes no allowance for the time it takes for the pilot to take physical action upon receiving any visual information.

A pilot maintaining a good lookout typically requires 12 or more seconds to recognise a potential collision threat, process the information, and take evasive action. This time includes the perception and decision-making process.

Evasive Action 

Seeing and avoiding other aircraft, even when you know their general location, isn’t always a quick and easy process.

Once an object of interest has been detected, a pilot still needs to identify it, determine if it’s a potential threat, and if required, make the necessary control movements and allow the aircraft to respond. Research has estimated that the time from recognition to evasive action is around 12.5 seconds. That may increase for less experienced pilots, older pilots, and those with less than optimal vision.

It is important to remember that if another aircraft appears to have no relative motion, it is likely to be on a collision course with you. If the other aircraft shows no horizontal or vertical motion on the windshield, but is increasing in size, take immediate evasive action.


10.18.20 Explain the see and avoid method of avoiding mid-air collisions. 

In Visual Meteorological conditions the pilot in command is responsible for looking out for other aircraft and avoiding mid-air collisions.

The physical limitations of the human eye are such that even the most careful search does not guarantee that traffic will be sighted.

Numerous limitations, including those of the human visual system, the demands of cockpit tasks, and various physical and environmental conditions combine to make see-and-avoid an uncertain method of traffic separation.

Pilots are 5 times more likely to have a midair collision with an aircraft flying in the same direction than with one flying in the opposite direction.
