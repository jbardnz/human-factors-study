10.64 First Aid



10.64.2 Describe the basic principles of first aid.

First aid is the initial care of the sick or injured. It can prevent the worsening of a condition and promote recovery if executed correctly. First aid lasts until medical aid arrives or until the casualty recovers.



First aid treatment shall be performed rapidly and efficiently using a five step problem solving approach:

1. Assess casualty.
2. Identify major problems.
3. Implement first aid skills.
4. Constantly reassess for response to treatment or for development of new problems.
5. If in doubt about diagnosis, give basic supportive measures and call for further assistance.




10.64.4 Describe the basic principles of Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation.

CARDIOPULMONARY RESUSCITATION (CPR) is a form of resuscitation used to keep a heart beating and sending oxygen to vital organs. It involves a method of mouth-to-mouth breathing and external cardiac compression.

The steps of CPR include:

  1. Check that the area is safe for you and your patient, and don appropriate Personal Protection Equipment
  2. Check for alertness/responsiveness with a tap of the shoulder
  3. Activate or (direct someone to activate) 111 or call backup as appropriate, also if not already available bring or have someone bring an AED to the patients side.
  4. Place the person on their back on a hard surface.
  5. Open the patients airway with a head tilt to slightly lift the chin.
  6. Simultaneously assess for carotid pulse in adults - you are feeling for the common carotid artery that's in a groove between the trachea and the neck muscles and is backed by the anterior surface of the cervical vertebrae. To palpate a carotid pulse: Place the tips of two to three fingers, (usually your index, middle, and ring finger if you use three) in the centre of the throat over the trachea. Slide your fingers towards yourself into the groove between the trachea and the neck muscles. Press your fingers gently into the groove. You may need to adjust the position of your fingers slightly up or down to find the pulse. Avoid moving into the upper third of the neck, as you could press on the carotid sinus which may lead to vagal stimulation/slowing the heart rate, particularly in older adults. Do not press too firmly, as you may cut off blood flow to the brain if the patient does not have good bilateral blood supply. ( check the brachial pulse in children and infants - inside of the elbow) and breathing for no more than 10 seconds.
  7. If patient is unresponsive and is not breathing or has abnormal breathing (ie. Agonal breathing) and no definitive pulse was, immediately begin chest compressions of at least 2 inches in depth (≥ 1/3 the anterior-posterior depth of the chest in children or infants) at a rate of 100-120 compressions per minute, allowing for full chest recoil.
  8. After performing 30 compressions administer two (2) rescue breaths, mouth to mouth, mouth to mask or through stoma as appropriate. For children and infants, if there are two rescuers performing CPR, the ratio of compressions to ventilations changes to 15:2.
  9. After the two rescue breaths have been administered immediately resume chest compressions.
  10. Repeat the cycle of 30 chest compressions and two rescue breaths and use the AED/Defibrillator as soon as it is available. Continue CPR until properly relieved by rescue personnel.

If additional personnel are available, switch compressors approximately every 4-5 cycles of 30 compressions and two breaths (approximately 2 minutes) to prevent rescuer fatigue and decreased compression effectiveness.
