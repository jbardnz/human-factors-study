10.38 Stress Management


10.38.2 Define Stress

Everyone is subjected to stress.

Stress is an excess of environmental demands over an individual’s capability to meet them. Living life places certain demands or STRESSORS on a person.

As long as a pilot’s abilities are greater than the demands placed on him or her operating an aircraft in the flying environment, stress should not become a problem.

If the pilot’s ability is NOT sufficient to meet the environmental demands then degraded performance levels and unnecessary risk taking may result.

It is important therefore that a pilot should not attempt anything that is beyond his or her capabilities.


10.38.4 Describe a simple model of stress. 

Some form of stress is essential to human survival and normal functioning. The stressors of every day life can be direct or indirect.


ALL OF THESE STRESSORS ARE CONSIDERED CUMULATIVE.
THEIR EFFECT IS ADDITIVE .

Indirect Stressors

Indirect stressors include those related to the general work situation, home and family, social and personal circumstances etc.

Indirect Stressors For A Pilot

Indirect stressors include those related to:

THE GENERAL WORK SITUATION

- Job Satisfaction, Career Development, Work Relationships, and Organisational Structure

HOME AND FAMILY

- Marital Problems, Deaths, Births, Moving House, Finances

SOCIAL AND PERSONAL CIRCUMSTANCES

 - Personality Needs, Values, Physical Condition



Stress from the pressures of everyday living can impair pilot performance, often in very subtle ways.

Difficulties, particularly at work can occupy thought processes enough to markedly decrease alertness.

Most pilots do not leave stress on the ground.

Certain emotionally upsetting events, including a serious argument, death of a family member, separation or divorce, loss of job and financial catastrophe can render a pilot unsafe to fly an aircraft safely.

Responsible pilots should consider grounding themselves. Otherwise, the emotions of anger, depression and anxiety from such events not only decrease alertness but may also lead to taking risks that border on self-destruction.

THE ADDITIVE EFFECT OF STRESSES OF DAILY LIFE COMBINED WITH THOSE OF THE COCKPIT MAY RESULT IN OVERLOAD!

Direct Stressors (Environmental)

The two main types of stress occurring in the course of a pilot’s work are:

FLYING STRESS -  associated with being in an aircraft flying at altitude 

EG. NOISE,GLARE,VIBRATION,TURBULENCE, EFFECT OF ALTITUDE, DISORIENTATION ETC

OPERATING STRESS - associated with ensuring that the aircraft remains flying correctly and safely. 

EG. FLIGHT PLANNING, ENROUTE NAVIGATING, POOR WEATHER, LOOKOUT, EMERGENCIES

In the context of psychology, arousal is the state of being physiologically alert, awake, and attentive

Psychological characteristics of arousal include feelings of vigor, energy, and tension.


10.38.8 Explain the relationship between stress and arousal. 

In the context of psychology, arousal is the state of being physiologically alert, awake, and attentive Psychological characteristics of arousal include feelings of vigor, energy, and tension.

Arousal And Overload

If mental workload is too high, or too much information is being received from the body sensors, or the level of mental activity is too great, the brain can be ‘overloaded’.

Arousal will be too high, so the pilot will begin to neglect some information or tasks, and normal functioning will begin to break down.

‘Load Shedding’ will automatically occur and problems and/or accidents will most likely follow.

There is an ideal level of mental activity/arousal. Too much stress or arousal is not good for a pilot, but nor is too little.




NOTE: PERFORMANCE IS LOW WHEN AROUSAL IS LOW
PERFORMANCE DECREASES WHEN AROUSAL IS TOO HIGH

Examples of physical, environmental, task-related, organisational and psychological stressors. 

Physical: Temperature, lighting, vibrations, ventilation

Environmental: Noise, glare, turbulence, altitude, disorientation

Task-related : Flight planning, enroute navigation, weather, lookout, emergencies

Organisational:  work overload, role conflict, job insecurity, monitoring, resource inadequacy


Psychological: Marital Problems, Deaths, Births, Moving House, Finances



34.38.10 Describe how the following environmental stressors affect pilot performance:

(a) heat; 

In temperatures above 35°C the body tries to keep its temperature below 37°C by increasing perspiration rate, heart rate and blood pressure. Attention becomes narrowed and restricted affecting your decision making 

(b) cold; 

The body sends blood to body core, toes and fingers feel cold and muscles begin to feel stiff and weak. You begin to feel tired and drowsy and start to shiver.

(c) noise; 

Excessive noise especially at high pitch can cause stress and fatigue. Above 80 dB it is recommended you wear ear protection to protect ears against stress damage.

(d) vibration; 

Vibrations transmitted to the body from the aircraft via the seat, seat belts and floor can make you feel uncomfortable and distract your from your main tasks.

(e) humidity. 

The importance of humidity in human heat exchange lies in its effect on evaporation of sweat. When humidity is high the ambient vapor pressure approaches that of moist skin and evaporation is greatly reduced. Thus, this avenue for heat loss is essentially closed, even though large quantities of sweat are produced. 

The maintenance of high sweat rates leads to progressive dehydration which is accompanied by impairment of mental and physical performance, and of heat dissipation. The intake of fluid during the work and leisure periods to replace sweat losses is imperative. As long as the humidity is low, relatively high environmental temperatures can be tolerated.

10.38.14 Explain the difference between acute and chronic stress. 

Stress can be ACUTE in the sense it is immediate and disappears after a short time. Or it can be CHRONIC, lasting longer and resulting in fatigue.

So examples of acute stress would be any stress you suffer from for a short period of time like a traffic jam, an argument with your spouse, an unkind criticism from your boss.

However if you are a bus driver or a cop and you get stuck in numerous traffic jams every day, or if you are in a bad relationship and you argue with your spouse constantly, or you work for a toxic boss - all the examples of acute stress cited above can potentially transform into examples of chronic stress.

Acute stress produces a high state of psychological and physiological arousal. The pilot will tend to focus on one problem, while ignoring more critical information. Situational awareness is severely diminished.

When a particularly difficult flying situation is faced (acute stress) by a pilot who is also under chronic stress from personal situations and normal cockpit duties that are often demanding, the potential exists for a stress overload with dangerous consequences.


34.38.16 Describe the psychological effects of stress.

Psychological effects of stress - the warning signs

Observations, either by the individual or other people that someone is under stress can be many and varied. Signs include for example:
  • MAKING MISTAKES
  • BECOMING SHORT TEMPERED
  • FORGETTING IMPORTANT TASKS (LOAD SHEDDING)
  • STARTING SMOKING OR SMOKING EXCESSIVELY
  • DRINKING ALCOHOL EXCESSIVELY
  • DISPLAYING NOISY OR UNCHARACTERISTIC BEHAVIOUR
  • HAVING FREQUENT CONFLICTS W I TH OTHER PEOPLE
  • DEVELOPING MARITAL PROBLEMS
  • BECOMING INVOLVED WITH THE LAW
  • FLYING DANGEROUSLY
  • REVERTING TO PREVIOUSLY LEARNT MORE BASIC SKILLS & DRILL

34.38.18 Describe the physiological effects of stress. 

Physiological effects of stress

Symptoms are changes from normal. The symptoms a pilot may begin to experience when stress levels are increasing include:
  • NERVOUSNESS OR SHAKING OF THE HANDS
  • SWEATING FOREHEAD AND/OR PALMS
  • AWARENESS OF THE HEART BEATING
  • OVER BREATHING
  • FEELING IRRITABLE


If the stress becomes very acute or severe the pilot may begin to have a break down or begin to act out certain non-typical behaviours. Acting out can include non-characteristic tough guy or macho displays, in an attempt to hide the
level of stress within.

IF THE STRESS OCCURS OVER A LONG TIME (STRESS x TIME) AND NO ALLOWANCE IS MADE FOR IT, FATIGUE WILL USUALLY SET IN .

                




10.38.20 Describe the factors that improve personal stress tolerance. 

Factors that influence your stress tolerance level include:

Your support network. A strong network of supportive friends and family members is an enormous buffer against stress. When you have people you can count on, life's pressures don't seem as overwhelming. On the flip side, the lonelier and more isolated you are, the greater your risk of succumbing to stress.

Your sense of control. If you have confidence in yourself and your ability to influence events and persevere through challenges, it's easier to take stress in stride. On the other hand, if you believe that you have little control over your life—that you're at the mercy of your environment and circumstances—stress is more likely to knock you off course.

Your attitude and outlook. The way you look at life and its inevitable challenges makes a huge difference in your ability to handle stress. If you're generally hopeful and optimistic, you'll be less vulnerable. Stress-hardy people tend to embrace challenges, have a stronger sense of humor, believe in a higher purpose, and accept change as an inevitable part of life.

Your ability to deal with your emotions. If you don't know how to calm and soothe yourself when you're feeling sad, angry, or troubled, you're more likely to become stressed and agitated. Having the ability to identify and deal appropriately with your emotions can increase your tolerance to stress and help you bounce back from adversity.

Your knowledge and preparation. The more you know about a stressful situation, including how long it will last and what to expect, the easier it is to cope. For example, if you go into surgery with a realistic picture of what to expect post-op, a painful recovery will be less stressful than if you were expecting to bounce back immediately.


10.38.22 Describe the relationship between stress and fatigue. 

Fatigue is the accumulation of stress.

Fatigue can be a major problem for pilots and occurs because of unresolved stress building up over a period.

ACUTE FATIGUE is short lasting. It arises from emergency conditions or particularly intense adverse circumstances - e.g. Engine Failure

Acute stress produces a high state of psychological and physiological arousal. The pilot will tend to focus on one problem, while ignoring more critical information. Situational awareness is severely diminished.

CHRONIC FATIGUE is the cumulative build-up of tension or depression resulting from a multiplicity of stressors in a person’s daily life. It gradually builds up over a period of weeks or months, and may take a complete rest or a change of environment to overcome.

When a particularly difficult flying situation is faced (acute stress) by a pilot who is also under chronic stress from personal situations and normal cockpit duties that are often demanding, the potential exists for a stress overload with dangerous consequences.


10.38.24 Explain methods of managing stress. 

                                                                                                    

The best ways to reduce stress and its additive effects are to:
  • KEEP PHYSICALLY FIT
  • KNOW ONE’S OWN CAPABILITIES AND LIMITATIONS AND TRY TO MATCH THESE TO ENVIRONMENTAL DEMANDS
  • THINK AND PLAN AHEAD
  • LEARN TO PRIORITISE
  • SEEK ASSISTANCE WHEN REQUIRED
  • AVOID PROCRASTINATION
  • ENSURE ADEQUATE DIET/FOOD, FLUID, REST, TIME OFF, RECREATION AND SLEEP
  • AVOID DEHYDRATION (THIS CAN CAUSE HEADACHE – WHICH I S A SWELLING OF THE CAPILLARY VESSELS I N THE BRAIN )
  • AVOID FLYING WHEN ILL OR ON DRUGS
  • AVOID SELF MEDICATION
  • AVOID BECOMING A WORKAHOLIC
  • AVOID SELF IMPOSED STRESSORS (SMOKING OR ALCOHOL)
  • LEARN RELAXATION TECHNIQUES
  • UNDERSTAND STRESS AND DO SOMETHING ABOUT IT.



Here are some practical examples;

REMOVE THE CAUSE
  • Noise – Get a good headset
  • Domestic Unhappiness – Talk it over with partner, get counselling
  • Turbulence – turn back and/or land
  • Undercarriage problem – Go around
  • Chronic Long-term Stress – Change Lifestyle/ Talk to people.
